6. describe kabuki theater ​


Answer:

What is Kabuki theater?
Kabuki is a classical form of Japanese dance-drama. Kabuki theatre is known for its heavily-stylised performances, the often-glamorous costumes worn by performers, and for the elaborate kumadori make-up worn by some of its performers.

where does Kabuki theater come from?

Kabuki comes from the verb kabuku, meaning "to slant or to sway". Kabuki is an art form characterized by colorful costumes, music, and stylized performances. Westerners first encountered Kabuki in the 19th century and early 20th century. Kabuki has been used to describe political drama and pageantry in the United States, Japan, and elsewhere.

Who uses Kabuki Theater?
Kabuki theater is still commonly used by Western political pundits across the political spectrum. Kabuki, Kabuki theater, and Kabuki dance are used interchangeably in punditry. Some have argued that this use of Kabuki is a misunderstanding of the artform, contrasting the form’s subtleties and nuances with the political meaning of “empty show.” However, some commentators do use Kabuki to indicate the complexities of political performance as opposed to a shorthand for “style over substance.”

14. 2 unique features of kabuki3 musical instruments used in Kabuki ​


Explanation:

Two unique features of Kabuki, a traditional Japanese theater form, are:

1. Onnagata: One distinct feature of Kabuki is the use of male actors to portray female roles, known as onnagata. These actors undergo extensive training to master the mannerisms, gestures, and speech patterns of women. They often wear elaborate costumes and makeup to enhance the femininity of their characters. Onnagata performances are highly stylized and showcase a unique blend of beauty, grace, and theatricality.

2. Keshō-mawashi: Kabuki actors make use of vibrant and elaborate costumes, including a special type of apron called keshō-mawashi. The keshō-mawashi is a wide, pleated skirt worn over the kimono. It is often brightly colored and adorned with intricate designs and patterns. The keshō-mawashi adds a visual spectacle to the performance and helps to distinguish the characters and their roles.

Three musical instruments commonly used in Kabuki performances are:

1. Shamisen: The shamisen is a three-stringed musical instrument with a long neck and a square-shaped body covered in skin. It is plucked with a plectrum, and its distinct sound adds depth and emotion to Kabuki performances. The shamisen is often played to accompany singing, dance, and dramatic scenes.

2. Taiko: Taiko drums are large traditional Japanese drums that produce powerful and resonant sounds. They are played with sticks called bachi and are used to create rhythmic patterns and add intensity and excitement to Kabuki performances. Taiko drums come in various sizes and are often played in ensembles to create dynamic and synchronized beats.

3. Nohkan: The nohkan is a bamboo flute specifically used in Noh and Kabuki theaters. It has a haunting and melodic sound that enhances the atmosphere and brings a sense of tradition and spirituality to the performance. The nohkan is played by skilled flutists who carefully control its tone and pitch to match the mood of the scene.

These instruments, along with others such as drums, bells, and string instruments, contribute to the rich and immersive musical experience in Kabuki theater.
