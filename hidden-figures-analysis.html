3. SYNOPSIS of the movie "Hidden Figures​


Answer:

"Hidden Figures" is a 2016 biographical drama film directed by Theodore Melfi, based on the non-fiction book of the same name by Margot Lee Shetterly. The film tells the true story of three African American women who worked at NASA during the 1960s space race and contributed to the successful launch of astronaut John Glenn into orbit.

The main characters are Katherine Goble Johnson (Taraji P. Henson), Dorothy Vaughan (Octavia Spencer), and Mary Jackson (Janelle Monáe). Katherine is a brilliant mathematician who is assigned to the Space Task Group to calculate the trajectories for the Mercury program. Dorothy is a supervisor in the West Area Computers division, where African American women are segregated and treated unfairly. Mary is a talented engineer who wants to become an engineer, but is held back by racial and gender discrimination.

The three women face numerous obstacles in their pursuit of success, including racist attitudes from their colleagues, lack of support from their families, and institutionalized discrimination. Despite these challenges, they persevere and make significant contributions to the space program.

The film also explores the broader cultural and political context of the 1960s, including the Civil Rights Movement and the Cold War. It highlights the role of African American women in the space program, and the importance of diversity and inclusion in science and technology.

"Hidden Figures" was a critical and commercial success, receiving numerous award nominations and grossing over $236 million worldwide. The film was praised for its powerful performances, inspiring story, and message of perseverance and equality.

Conformity is the act of matching attitudes, beliefs, and behaviors to group norms, politics or being like-minded.[1] Norms are implicit, specific rules, shared by a group of individuals, that guide their interactions with others. People often choose to conform to society rather than to pursue personal desires - because it is often easier to follow the path others have made already, rather than forging a new one. This tendency to conform occurs in small groups and/or in society as a whole, and may result from subtle unconscious influences (predisposed state of mind), or from direct and overt social pressure. Conformity can occur in the presence of others, or when an individual is alone. For example, people tend to follow social norms when eating or when watching television, even if alone.

People often[quantify] conform from a desire for security within a group, also known as normative influence[2]—typically a group of a similar age, culture, religion or educational status. This is often referred to[by whom?] as groupthink: a pattern of thought characterized by self-deception, forced manufacture of consent, and conformity to group values and ethics, which ignores realistic appraisal of other courses of action. Unwillingness to conform carries the risk of social rejection. Conformity is often associated in media with adolescence and youth culture, but strongly affects humans of all ages.[3]

Although peer pressure may manifest negatively, conformity can be regarded[by whom?] as either good or bad. Driving on the conventionally-approved side of the road may be seen as beneficial conformity.[4] With the appropriate environmental influence, conforming, in early childhood years, allows one to learn and thus, adopt the appropriate behaviours necessary to interact and develop "correctly" within one's society.[5] Conformity influences the formation and maintenance of social norms, and helps societies function smoothly and predictably via the self-elimination of behaviors seen as contrary to unwritten rules.[6]

As conformity is a group phenomenon, factors such as group size, unanimity, cohesion, status, prior commitment and public opinion help determine the level of conformity an individual displays.

25. what risky acts of courage do Katherine,Mary and Dorothy take?How are they rewarded? from the movie hidden figures​


Answer:

In the movie Hidden Figures, Katherine, Mary, and Dorothy all take risky acts of courage in order to achieve their goals and overcome the discrimination and prejudice they face as African American women working in the field of mathematics and aerospace engineering during the 1960s.

Katherine, a mathematician, takes the risk of standing up to her white male colleagues and supervisor to prove her capabilities and demand equal pay and opportunities. She is rewarded with a promotion to supervisor and becomes an integral part of the team working on the launch of John Glenn into orbit.

Mary, an engineer, takes the risk of leaving her position at the National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics (NACA) to work at NASA, despite the resistance she faces from her colleagues and the limited opportunities for African American women at the time. She is rewarded with the opportunity to lead the team that calculates the trajectories for Alan Shepard's Freedom 7 mission.

Dorothy, a human computer, takes the risk of fighting for the right to be trained on IBM machines and to be recognized as a computer programmer. She is rewarded with a promotion to supervisor and becomes a pioneer in the field of computer programming.

Overall, Katherine, Mary, and Dorothy are rewarded for their courage with greater opportunities and recognition in their respective fields, and they make significant contributions to the United States' space program.
