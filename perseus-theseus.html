5. the ship of theseus is an artifact in a museum. Over time its wooden planks would rot and would be replaced by new planks. when no original planks remain, is it still the ship of theseus?​


Explanation:

This is a classic philosophical thought experiment known as the Ship of Theseus paradox. The paradox raises the question of whether an object that has had all its components replaced over time remains fundamentally the same object.

In the case of the Ship of Theseus, it is difficult to determine whether it is still the same ship if all its wooden planks have been replaced. Some may argue that it is no longer the same ship since none of the original planks remain. Others may argue that it is still the same ship since it retains the same form and function.

Ultimately, the answer to this question may depend on one's definition of identity and whether it is based on the physical components of an object or its overall form and function.

19. What is similarities medusa and perseus?what difference medusawhat difference perseus​


Answer:

Medusa: The head or face of Medusa was a common feature of Greek pottery, art, and architecture, and is known as a Gorgoneion. What was so unique about her depiction is that more often than not she was depicted facing front. This is quite uncommon compared to most other ancient Greek art, where faces were depicted in profile.

Perseus: Perseus, in Greek mythology, the slayer of the Gorgon Medusa and the rescuer of Andromeda from a sea monster. Perseus was the son of Zeus and Danaë, the daughter of Acrisius of Argos. As an infant he was cast into the sea in a chest with his mother by Acrisius, to whom it had been prophesied that he would be killed by his grandson. After Perseus had grown up on the island of Seriphus, where the chest had grounded, King Polydectes of Seriphus, who desired Danaë, tricked Perseus into promising to obtain the head of Medusa, the only mortal among the Gorgons.
