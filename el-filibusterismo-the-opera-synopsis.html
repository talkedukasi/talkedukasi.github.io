2. write synopsis of El Filibusterismo​


Answer:

Juan Crisostomo Ibarra has changed his name and identity to a rich jeweler named Simoun. In disguise, he travels the world amassing wealth, which he intends to use to topple the corrupt and abusive regime in his native land. But Simoun's real motive is personal. He wants to rescue his beloved, Maria Clara, from the convent and avenge the death of his father. In a forest on Christmas Eve, Basilio, a medical student recognizes Simoun's true identity as the man who helped bury his mother Sisa more than ten years before, under a tree. Simoun is in the forest to retrieve the gems he buried near Sisa's grave. As the film progresses, all the secrets and intrigues are revealed, and the plotters' plan fails. A good Filipino priest throws Simoun's jewels away, thereby eliminating the greed, violence and other evils they might have provoked.

5. a synopsis of carmen in opera in romantic period


Carmen is an opera by Georges Bizet based on an 1845 novella by French dramatist. The title character, a wild Spanish gypsy, is unscrupulous in matters of the law and of the heart.

The opening scene is set in the 1830's in Seville, Spain. Carmen attracts everyone's attention, but soldier Don Jose pretends not to notice her. The flirtatious Carmen throws him a flower. He intends to throw it away, but hides it when his girlfriend, Micaela, arrives. A knife fight breaks out between Carmen and another woman, and Jose is ordered to arrest Carmen. Thinking fast, Carmen seduces Jose and escapes, which results in Jose's arrest for breach of duty.

A month later, Carmen and her friends are entertaining soldiers in a tavern. A famous bullfighter, Escamillo, arrives. He flirts with Carmen, who rebuffs him because she is waiting for José to be released from jail. When Jose arrives, Carmen rewards him with an erotic dance. As he prepares to return to the barracks for the night, she taunts him, saying that if he really loved her, he would run off with her. Jose protests and shows her that he has saved the flower she gave him as a sign of true love. At that moment, Jose's lieutenant returns to arrest Carmen. The men fight and are separated by Carmen's gang of smugglers. Because Jose has attacked a superior officer, he has no choice but to run away with Carmen and the smugglers to the mountains.

In the mountains, Carmen bores of Jose and advises him to go home to his mother. Escamillo arrives and invites everyone to his next bullfight before he leaves. The closing act depicts Escamillo's bullfight. Carmen is on Escamillo's arm, but she runs into José outside the arena. Jose begs Carmen to come away with him, but she refuses, saying that she was born free and will die free. In a jealous rage, Jose stabs her to death while the crowd inside the arena cheers Escamillo to victory.

16. explain briefly about noli me tangere and el filibusterismo as an example of an opera (ASAP)​


Explanation:

"Noli Me Tangere" and "El Filibusterismo" are not operas, but rather novels written by Dr. Jose Rizal during the Spanish colonial period in the Philippines. These two novels are considered as two of the most important works of Philippine literature and played a significant role in Philippine history.

"Noli Me Tangere" was published in 1887 and is a story about the social and political issues experienced by Filipinos during the Spanish colonization in the Philippines. The novel exposes the injustices committed by the Spanish friars and highlights the struggles of the Filipino people during that time. The title "Noli Me Tangere" is a Latin phrase that means "Touch Me Not," which symbolizes the protagonist's warning to his fellow Filipinos to never touch the fragile social fabric of their society.

"El Filibusterismo," published in 1891, is the sequel to "Noli Me Tangere." It continues the story of the protagonist, Crisostomo Ibarra, now under the identity of Simoun, four years after the events of the first novel. It explores the themes of revolution and how Filipinos were pushed to rebellion due to the injustices they experienced. The title "El Filibusterismo" comes from the word "filibustero," which refers to a person who engages in rebellion or insurrection.

Although they are not operas, both novels have been adapted into various forms of media, including stage plays, movies, and musical productions. The themes of social injustices, rebellion, and the struggles of the Filipino people during the Spanish colonial period continue to resonate with Filipinos today.
