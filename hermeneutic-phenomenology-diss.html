Hermeneutical phenomenology is a type of qualitative research that looks at the interpretation of meaning through the lived experiences of participants. It focuses on the interrelationships between the subjective and the objective, and considers how the social, cultural, and historical contexts in which individuals live shape how they experience and make sense of the world. In essence, it views the grasp of meaning as something developed in dialogue between the individual and his or her environment. As such, socio-cultural conditions are seen as a central factor in forming hermeneutical phenomenology, which partly explains why the approach has a particular affinity with cultural anthropology and, most notably, the work of Edward Sapir and Claude Lévi-Strauss.

Central to this approach is taking the interpretation of meaning seriously in its own right, to the point where the researcher is encouraged to explore how physical, emotional, and behavioral responses arise as a result of the interplay between personal cognitive processing and the cultural, societal and structural aspects of the environment. This means focusing on how the broader socio-cultural landscape shapes how people think and how they act, as well as their relationship with the world and other people. Ultimately, the aim is to close the gap between the subjective and the objective by focusing on our individual understandings and experiences within context.

Answer:

In hermeneutical phenomenology, socio-cultural conditions refer to the broader cultural and social contexts in which individuals exist and which shape their experiences and understanding of the world. These conditions can include factors such as historical events, language, cultural norms and values, power structures, and social identities.

Socio-cultural conditions are important to consider in hermeneutical phenomenology because they help us to understand how individuals make sense of their experiences and how their experiences are shaped by the larger social and cultural contexts in which they live. By examining the socio-cultural conditions that shape an individual's understanding of the world, we can gain a deeper understanding of their experiences and how they interpret and give meaning to those experiences.

For example, an individual's experiences of discrimination may be shaped by larger social and cultural factors such as systemic racism, cultural norms and values, and historical events. By examining these socio-cultural conditions, we can gain a deeper understanding of how these experiences are interpreted and understood by the individual and how they impact their sense of self and their relationship to the world around them.

Overall, socio-cultural conditions are an important consideration in hermeneutical phenomenology because they help us to understand how individuals interpret and give meaning to their experiences within the broader social and cultural contexts in which they exist.

How phenomenology can help us learn from the experiences of others

Brian E. Neubauer,corresponding author1,2 Catherine T. Witkop,3 and Lara Varpio1

Author information Copyright and License information Disclaimer

This article has been cited by other articles in PMC.

Go to:

Abstract

Introduction

As a research methodology, phenomenology is uniquely positioned to help health professions education (HPE) scholars learn from the experiences of others. Phenomenology is a form of qualitative research that focuses on the study of an individual’s lived experiences within the world. Although it is a powerful approach for inquiry, the nature of this methodology is often intimidating to HPE researchers. This article aims to explain phenomenology by reviewing the key philosophical and methodological differences between two of the major approaches to phenomenology: transcendental and hermeneutic. Understanding the ontological and epistemological assumptions underpinning these approaches is essential for successfully conducting phenomenological research.

Purpose

This review provides an introduction to phenomenology and demonstrates how it can be applied to HPE research. We illustrate the two main sub-types of phenomenology and detail their ontological, epistemological, and methodological differences.

Conclusions

Phenomenology is a powerful research strategy that is well suited for exploring challenging problems in HPE. By building a better understanding of the nature of phenomenology and working to ensure proper alignment between the specific research question and the researcher’s underlying philosophy, we hope to encourage HPE scholars to consider its utility when addressing their research questions.

About phenomenology

Phenomenology is a study in terms of philosophy that studies human as a phenomenon. The science of phenomonology in philosophy is usually associated with the science of hermeneutics, namely the science that studies the benefits of this phenomenon.

This term was first introduced by Johann Heinrich Lambert (1728 - 1777), a German philosopher. In his book Neues Organon (1764). he wrote about unreal knowledge.

In the literary approach, phenomenology uses the intuitive experience of phenomena, something that has a phenomenological reflection, as a starting point and effort to get to the nature of experience and the nature of what we experience. G.W.F. Hegel and Edmund Husserl are two important figures in the development of this philosophical approach.

The phenomenological tradition concentrates on personal experience including the realm of individuals who actually share experiences with one another. Communication is seen as a round of sharing experiences or information between individuals through discussion. Good relations between individuals have a high position in this tradition. In this tradition, it is said that language represents a meaning for objects. So, just one word can give meaning to something that will be interpreted.

Fundamentally, phenomenology is a tradition of study used to explore human experience. As stated by Littlejohn that phenomenology is a tradition to explore human experience. In this context, the assumption is correct that humans actively understand the world around them as a life experience and actively interpret that experience.

The main assumption of phenomenology is that humans actively interpret their experiences by providing a formula for something they experience. Therefore interpretation is an active process to provide a formula for something that is experienced by humans. In other words understanding is a creative action, namely action towards meaning.
