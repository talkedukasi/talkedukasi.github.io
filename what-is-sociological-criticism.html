1. What are the techniques in sociological criticism?


DEFINITION
Sociological criticism is literary criticism directed to understanding (or placing) literature in its larger social context; it codifies the literary strategies that are employed to represent social constructs through a sociological methodology. Sociological criticism analyzes both how the social functions in literature and how literature works in society. This form of literary criticism was introduced by Kenneth Burke, a 20th-century literary and critical theorist, whose article "Literature As Equipment for Living" outlines the specification and significance of such a critique.

TECHNIQUES
Sociological criticism focuses on the relationship between literature and society, the social function of literature. Literature is always produced in a social context. Writers may affirm or criticize the values of the society in which they live, but they write for an audience and that audience is society. Sociological criticism is literary criticism directed to understanding (or placing) literature in its larger social context; it codifies the literary strategies that are employed to represent social constructs through a sociological methodology. Definitions of key terms for the five basic sociological perspectives – Functionalism, Marxism, Feminism, Social Action Theory and Postmodernism. The sociological approach goes beyond everyday common sense. ... Sociology, is an attempt to understand the social world by situating social events in their corresponding environment (i.e., social structure, culture, history) and trying to understand social phenomena by collecting and analyzing empirical data.

The male characters in the story view the roosters as extensions of themselves, and derive pleasure from the brutal fights.

The narrator idolizes his father and grandfather, who are both involved in cockfighting, and feels the need to prove himself to them. The violent nature of the sport also serves as an outlet for the emotions that the male characters struggle to express in a healthy manner.

From a sociological criticism perspective, the story highlights the cultural significance of cockfighting in certain communities. The rural setting and the men's devotion to the sport can be seen as a representation of the traditions and values that are deeply ingrained in certain regions. The story also brings to light the issue of animal cruelty and the ethical implications of participating in this type of activity.

Overall, "All these Fighting Cocks" portrays the harmful effects of toxic masculinity and the cultural significance of certain traditions, while also shedding light on the issue of animal cruelty.

Answer:

Historical context is an important part of life and literature, and without it, memories, stories, and characters have less meaning. Historical context deals with the details that surround an occurrence. In more technical terms, historical context refers to the social, religious, economic, and political conditions that existed during a certain time and place. Basically, it's all the details of the time and place in which a situation occurs, and those details are what enable us to interpret and analyze works or events of the past, or even the future, rather than merely judge them by contemporary standards.

In literature, a strong understanding of the historical context behind a work's creation can give us a better understanding of and appreciation for the narrative. In analyzing historical events, context can help us understand what motivates people to behave as they did.

27. EXPLAIN THE PHENOMENA OF FESTIVAL USING THE STRUCTURAL FUNCTIONALISM, INTERPRETIVE AND CRITICAL PERSPECTIVE OF SOCIOLOGY. ​.


Answer:

Structural Functionalism: Festivals are an important part of social cohesion, as they bring people together to celebrate a common culture and values. They provide a sense of shared identity, a sense of belonging, and contribute to social stability. The various activities that take place during a festival encourage friendship and mutual understanding, strengthen trust and create a sense of unity.

Interpretive Perspective: Festivals are a way for people to express themselves and their values. They can be used as a means of shedding traditional status hierarchies and embracing creativity, new ideas, and cultural exchange. The performance, display, and discussion of various art forms can provide a way to challenge existing norms and form new collective understandings.

Critical Perspective: Festivals can act as a form of societal control by reinforcing dominant values and suppressing minority perspectives. They can be used to celebrate a certain power structure or a particular version of history as well as to discredit cultural practices seen as inappropriate or politically unpopular. These events can be manipulated and used as a tool by the powerful to maintain their own interests.

Answer:

Festivals are a crucial component of social cohesiveness because they bring people together to celebrate a shared culture and set of values, according to structural functionalism. They promote social stability by fostering a sense of shared identity and belonging. A festival's numerous events foster unity, trust, and a sense of community while fostering friendship and intercultural understanding.

Interpretation: People can express who they are and what they believe in via festivals. They can be applied as a strategy for letting go of conventional status hierarchies and embracing innovation, fresh thinking, and cross-cultural communication. Different art forms can offer an opportunity to question social conventions and create new, shared understandings through performance, display, and discussion.
