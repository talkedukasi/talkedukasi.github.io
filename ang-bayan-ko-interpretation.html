"Bayan Ko," originally titled "Nuestra Patria" in Spanish (usually referred to as "My Country"), is one of the most familiar patriotic songs in the Philippines. The poet José Corazon de Jesus translated it into Tagalogo some thirty years ago in the Spanish version of the revolutionary general José Alejandrino in the light of the Philippines-American War and the resulting conquest of America. The tune, which is kundiman, is often regarded by the Filipino Overseas groups after or on its own as the second inofficial national anthem for the Philippines. Because of its renown, it is often considered folk music and because its lyrics are simplicity it is used in numerous parts of Philippine culture as a protest song by various political parties.

Origin

Bayan Ko's Spanish lyrics were initially composed for the Severino Reyes zarzuela, Walang Sugat ("no wound"). Attributed to the propagandist, General José Alejandrino, the song expressed opposition to the continuing American occupation. José Corazón de Jesús and Constancio de Guzmán receive the latest and more common version from Tagalog.

Protest song during the Marcos dictatorship

Under the Marcos dictatorship, Bayan Ko regained cult popularity, while the Left sang its own version in protest. Since President Marcos had imposed Martial Law in 1972, the song was considered seditious. Public performance of the song was banned, with violators facing possible prosecution and imprisonment. People were urged to sing it at the 1983 funeral of Senator Benigno Aquino Jr. and the subsequent 1986 People's Power Revolution, where Freddie Aguilar led the crowds to sing.  

Modern  

Since the 1986 Revolution that overthrew the Marcos administration and inaugurated the Fifth Republic, the song has been linked to anti-government demonstrations. In February 1987, the cover of pop star JoAnne Lorenzana was unveiled as part of the PLDT Nationalist Movement and broadcast on radio and television for the first anniversary of the Revolution.
