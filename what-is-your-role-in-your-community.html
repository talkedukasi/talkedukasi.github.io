20. what is your role as a responsible citizen of your community


Community responsibilities are an individual's duties or obligations to the community and include cooperation, respect and participation. The concept goes beyond thinking and acting as individuals to common beliefs about shared interests and life. A basic community responsibility is voting in elections.

A responsible citizen has knowledge about his/her role in community, state and the world. A responsible citizen has a role in making the world a better place to live (for every components in biosphere). A responsible citizen is change agent that acts out against injustice in social, economic, and environmental sectors.

Citizenship is the duty and responsibility as a member of a community and social responsibility is the duty of organisations in which they are held accountable for fulfilling their civic duty.

28. WHAT IS YOUR ROLE IN COMMUNITY IN YOUR COUNTRY?


Answer:

I was sitting in the passenger side of my friend Golf’s car. Suddenly, he said “I’m very lucky. Our whole town’s really lucky. Thai people don’t get opportunities like this.”

I was confused.

“What opportunity?” I replied.

He answered, “Knowing you. You are a blessing.”

I didn’t know how to reply.

In my community, I am seen as an opportunity. I am the connection to a better life. I am the chance at speaking English well. I am the ability to find needed information. I am the only foreigner anybody has ever known.

Other college students ask me how they can study in America if they don’t have any money. I connect them to scholarships at my old university, I contact professors to see if they have research positions available, I get them a way in. I offer them a place to stay if they ever find the time to visit.

For others, I’m the only chance they have at practicing English. English tutors are typically $30 an hour, a price which most here cannot afford. They see just speaking with me as a blessing.

For many, I am the only foreigner they’ve met who speaks Thai and hangs with Thai people. They tell me, “Foreigners never come here. They just go to the beaches and to Bangkok. We never get to speak with them.” They ask me many questions about America, like “Do you buy your bananas in groups or one-by-one?” and “Do people own mopeds in America?”, and my favorite, “Is everyone in America fat?” I am a source of real information they’ve never had access to before, a source of information that they’re all curious about.

Since very few people read or speak English well, I am the ability to obtain or put out information as well. I can research needed information not yet translated to the Thai language for them, I can ask websites for information because of my English ability, and I can correct their grammar for a professional appearance to the world community. They are very grateful.

Every room I walk into, everyone is excited that I’m there. People are honored that I would spend time with them and get to know them. I’m seen as a blessing wherever I go. I am the token foreigner. It’s a nice feeling.
