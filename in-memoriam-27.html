"Da-duh in Memoriam" is a short story written by the Caribbean writer Paule Marshall. The story follows a young girl who goes to visit her grandmother in Barbados, where she experiences a clash of cultures and perspectives.

One of the major thematic concerns in the story is the conflict between tradition and modernity. The grandmother, Da-duh, represents the traditional way of life in Barbados, while the girl represents modernity and the changes that are coming to the island. This conflict is reflected in the descriptions of the landscape and the people of Barbados, as well as in the attitudes and actions of the characters.

Another major thematic concern in the story is the struggle for identity and self-discovery. The girl is torn between her loyalty to her grandmother and her desire to explore the wider world and embrace new experiences. This struggle is embodied in the contrast between the lush, tropical landscape of Barbados and the concrete, industrial world of New York, where the girl lives.

Marshall employs a number of literary techniques to convey these thematic concerns, including symbolism, imagery, and point of view. For example, the character of Da-duh is portrayed as a powerful, imposing figure who represents the strength and resilience of traditional culture, while the girl is portrayed as a curious, questioning outsider who is eager to explore and learn. The descriptions of the natural world are rich and vivid, evoking the lushness and beauty of the Caribbean landscape, while the descriptions of the urban environment are stark and sterile, highlighting the contrast between the two worlds.

Overall, "Da-duh in Memoriam" is a powerful exploration of the clash of cultures and the struggle for identity and self-discovery that are central to the Caribbean experience.

3. Explain the thematic concerns explored by Caribbean women writers in Da-duh in Memoriam and The Draught by Sylvia Wynter.​


PA BRAINLIEST

Answer:

"Da-duh in Memoriam" by Paule Marshall and "The Draught" by Sylvia Wynter are two short stories written by Caribbean women writers that explore the experiences and struggles of Caribbean people, particularly women, in the colonial and postcolonial eras.

In "Da-duh in Memoriam," the story is narrated by a young girl who visits her grandmother in Barbados for the first time. The story explores the themes of identity, colonialism, and cultural conflict. The grandmother, Da-duh, is proud of her country and its independence from British colonialism, while the young girl is more interested in the modern American culture she is used to. Through their conversations, the story highlights the cultural divide between the old and the new, the traditional and the modern, and the struggles of coming to terms with one's identity.

In "The Draught," the story centers around a poor Jamaican family struggling to survive during a severe drought. The story explores themes of poverty, social injustice, and the struggles of everyday life in a society that is not always just. The story highlights the plight of women who are often forced to bear the brunt of poverty and social injustice, as well as the resilience of people who are able to find hope and strength in the face of adversity.

In both stories, the writers highlight the experiences of Caribbean women in a society that is often patriarchal and oppressive. They explore themes of identity, cultural conflict, poverty, and social injustice, and the struggles of Caribbean people to survive and find meaning in a changing world. These themes are still relevant today, as Caribbean people continue to navigate the challenges of postcolonialism and globalization.
