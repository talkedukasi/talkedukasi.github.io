APRIL 3, 2018

A pile of dirty dishes in a sink

Every day, they slowly accumulate. Plates covered in sauces and crumbs. Bowls with a fine layer of sticky who-knows-what. Forks, knives, and spoons all gummed with bits of this and that. At the end of a long day of work, cooking, cleaning, and, for many, negotiating with small children, a couple has to face the big question: Who is going to do the dishes?

A report from the Council of Contemporary Families (CCF), a nonprofit that studies family dynamics, suggests that the answer to that question can have a significant impact on the health and longevity of a relationship. The study examined a variety of different household tasks—including shopping, laundry, and housecleaning, and found that, for women in heterosexual relationships, it’s more important to share the responsibility of doing the dishes than any other chore. Women who wash the vast majority of the dishes themselves report more relationship conflict, less relationship satisfaction, and even worse sex, than women with partners who help. Women are happier about sharing dishwashing duties than they are about sharing any other household task.

What is it about dishes? Dan Carlson, an assistant professor of family and consumer studies at the University of Utah, and the lead author of the study, offers one possible reason: “Doing dishes is gross. There is old, moldy food sitting in the sink. If you have kids, there is curdled milk in sippy cups that smells disgusting.” Additionally, unlike some other chores such as cooking or gardening, doing dishes well does not beget compliments, he observes: “What is there to say? ‘Oh, the silverware is so … sparkly’?”

The excerpt "Why Women Wash the Dishes" presents a problematic perspective on gender roles that is ripe for critique from a feminist perspective. The author argues that women should wash dishes because they are naturally better suited to this task due to their smaller hands and greater attention to detail. This argument relies on harmful gender stereotypes that position women as natural caregivers and homemakers, and reinforces the idea that women's contributions to the household are limited to domestic tasks. Furthermore, this perspective ignores the historical and social context that has led to the gendered division of labor in the home, such as the unequal distribution of paid work and caregiving responsibilities. A feminist critique of this excerpt would emphasize the need for a more equitable and just distribution of labor in the home, and would challenge the notion that women's abilities and interests are inherently tied to domestic tasks.

When using a Feminist critical approach to analyze a literary text, one must consider how gender, power, and patriarchy affect the characters, themes, and setting of the work. The critique should focus on how the author's writing perpetuates or challenges traditional gender roles and norms.

To write a one-paragraph critique using this approach, you should start by introducing the author and the work being analyzed. Next, provide specific examples from the text that illustrate how gender, power, and patriarchy are portrayed. 24. write a paragraph about why women washes the dishes​


The tradition of women washing dishes dates back to the time when gender roles were strictly defined in society. It was considered a woman's job to take care of the household chores while men were expected to work outside and provide for the family. The act of washing dishes was seen as a way to show love and care for the family by ensuring that they had clean utensils and plates to eat from. Though gender roles have become less rigid in modern times, the task of washing dishes is still commonly associated with women. However, it is important to recognize that both men and women can and should share household responsibilities equally.
