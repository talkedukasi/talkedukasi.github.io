4. why was the republic established in the philippines by the japanese called the puppet republic ​


Background Edit

After the Japanese invasion of the Philippines, President Manuel L. Quezon had declared the national capital Manila an "open city", and left it under the rule of Jorge B. Vargas, as mayor. The Japanese entered the city on January 2, 1942, and established it as the capital. Japan fully captured the Philippines on May 6, 1942, after the Battle of Corregidor.

General Masaharu Homma decreed the dissolution of the Commonwealth of the Philippines and established the Philippine Executive Commission, a caretaker government, with Vargas as its first chairman in January 1942. KALIBAPI — Kapisanan sa Paglilingkod sa Bagong Pilipinas (Tagalog for the "Association for Service to the New Philippines") — was formed by Proclamation No. 109 of the Philippine Executive Commission (Komisyong Tagapagpaganap ng Pilipinas), a piece of legislation passed on December 8, 1942, banning all existing political parties and creating the new governing alliance. Its first director-general was Benigno Aquino, Sr.[2] The pro-Japanese Ganap Party, which saw the Japanese as the saviors of the archipelago, was absorbed into the KALIBAPI.[3]

Independence Edit
Before the formation of the Preparatory Commission, the Japanese gave an option to put the Philippines under the dictatorship of Artemio Ricarte, whom the Japanese returned from Yokohama to help bolster their propaganda movement. However, the Philippine Executive Commission refused this option and chose to make the Philippines a republic instead. During his first visit to the Philippines on May 6, 1943, Prime Minister Hideki Tōjō promised to return independence to the Philippines as part of its propaganda of Pan-Asianism (Asia for the Asians).[4]

This prompted the KALIBAPI to create the Preparatory Committee for Philippine Independence on June 19, 1943.[2] A draft constitution was formed by the Preparatory Commission for Independence, consisting of 20 members from the KALIBAPI.[5] The Preparatory Commission, led by José P. Laurel,[6] presented its draft Constitution on September 4, 1943, and three days later, the KALIBAPI general assembly ratified the draft Constitution.[5]

By September 20, 1943, the KALIBAPI's representative groups in the country's provinces and cities elected from among themselves fifty-four members of the Philippine National Assembly, the legislature of the country, with fifty-four governors and city mayors as ex-officio members.

9. why did civil wars often occur in the roman republic​


Answer:

greed of power

Explanation:

Let us get a little more sophisticated and parse these ‘civil wars’ a little.

First, there were true ‘civil wars’ where different factions of Rome battled for reconstruction of the Roman ‘constitution’ and/or the Roman politie. These civil wars were most like those we identify in the US 1861–65, Britain ca 1640, 1689, France 1790 et al, England 1141…

Then there were the civil wars of various ‘Emperors’- after Marius, Roman soldiers were paid through their commanders- who also determined when, where and how to loot (a substantial element in soldier’s pay). After the Julian line died with Nero, there was almost no dynastic element in Imperial succession. Without that tie, a dead or weak Emperor could generate a state of uncertainty about Imperial succession- a perfect storm for insurrection and civil war. There was nothing ideological in these conflicts, just, “My general has bigger or better cojones than yours… etc.” While these wars were inside the state, there was little ‘civil’ about them.

Last, but not least, were the civil wars between legitimate Emperors. Diocletian had ‘reformed’ and restructured the Imperial system with the result that there were now four Emperors- actually, two Emperors and two Caesars. Nobody but Diocletian could this work so every time one of the Quadrumvirate died the other leaders struggled to promote themselves.

Thus Rome had civil wars because it never established a well defined constitution nor a legitimate line of succession. Too many swords, too few brains.

15. what happened to republic of yemen after world war 2?​


Answer:

After World War II, the Republic of Yemen was established in North Yemen in 1962, after the overthrow of the Imamate, a theocratic monarchy that had ruled for over a thousand years. The country then experienced a period of political turmoil and economic instability. The government struggled to build a strong and centralized state, and was faced with various internal conflicts, including a civil war in 1962-1970.

In the south, British colonial rule ended in 1967, and the People's Democratic Republic of Yemen (PDRY) was established. The PDRY was a Marxist-Leninist state and was supported by the Soviet Union. The PDRY and the Yemen Arab Republic (YAR) in the north, engaged in a series of border disputes and sporadic military clashes throughout the 1970s and 1980s.

In 1990, the PDRY and YAR were united to form the Republic of Yemen. However, the unity was not smooth and the country faced significant economic and political challenges in the post-unification period. The government struggled to reconcile the different political and economic systems of the former north and south, and to address the demands of various groups who felt marginalized by the new government.

From 2011, Yemen was hit by a wave of protests, partly sparked by the Arab Spring and partly by longstanding grievances over unemployment, poverty and political marginalization. This led to the civil war in Yemen in 2015, which still ongoing, and the country is facing humanitarian crisis as a result of the ongoing conflict.

29. Explain the punic wars of the Roman republic and the Carthage


Answer:

Punic Wars, also called Carthaginian Wars, (264–146 bce), a series of three wars between the Roman Republic and the Carthaginian (Punic) empire, resulting in the destruction of Carthage, the enslavement of its population, and Roman hegemony over the western Mediterranean.

The conflict lasted for 23 years and caused substantial materiel and human losses on both sides; the Carthaginians were ultimately defeated by the Romans. By the terms of the peace treaty, Carthage paid large war reparations to Rome and Sicily fell to Roman control—thus becoming a Roman province.
