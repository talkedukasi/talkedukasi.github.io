5. computation of marginal revenue and marginal cost approach​


Answer:

Marginal revenue (MR) is the additional revenue a company earns by selling one additional unit of its product or service. Marginal cost (MC) is the additional cost a company incurs by producing one additional unit of its product or service.

The marginal revenue and marginal cost approach is used to determine the optimal level of production and pricing for a company. To compute marginal revenue and marginal cost, the following steps can be taken:

1. Calculate the total revenue (TR) from selling a certain number of units of a product or service.

2. Calculate the total cost (TC) of producing that same number of units.

3. Calculate the marginal revenue (MR) by subtracting the total revenue of the previous unit from the total revenue of the current unit.

4. Calculate the marginal cost (MC) by subtracting the total cost of the previous unit from the total cost of the current unit.

Once MR and MC are computed, the company can use them to make decisions about production and pricing.

If MR is greater than MC, the company should produce and sell more units because each additional unit produced will generate more revenue than cost. If MR is less than MC, the company should produce and sell fewer units because the additional cost of production exceeds the additional revenue generated.

Additionally, if the company wants to maximize its profit, it should continue producing and selling units until the point where MR equals MC. This is because at this point, the additional revenue generated by producing one more unit is exactly equal to the additional cost incurred, resulting in maximum profit.

The marginal revenue and marginal cost approach is a method used by businesses to determine the optimal level of production that will maximize profits. Here is an example to illustrate this approach:

Let's say a company produces and sells widgets. The cost of producing each widget is $5, and the company sells each widget for $10. The company wants to determine the optimal level of production that will maximize its profits.

To use the marginal revenue and marginal cost approach, the company needs to calculate its marginal revenue and marginal cost at different levels of production. Marginal revenue is the additional revenue generated by producing and selling one additional unit of the product. Marginal cost is the additional cost incurred by producing and selling one additional unit of the product.

Here is a table that shows the marginal revenue and marginal cost at different levels of production:

| Quantity | Total Revenue | Marginal Revenue | Total Cost | Marginal Cost |

|----------|--------------|-----------------|------------|---------------|

| 1 | $10 | $10 | $5 | $5 |

| 2 | $20 | $10 | $10 | $5 |

| 3 | $30 | $10 | $15 | $5 |

| 4 | $40 | $10 | $20 | $5 |

| 5 | $50 | $10 | $25 | $5 |

| 6 | $60 | $10 | $30 | $5 |

As we can see from the table, the marginal revenue remains constant at $10 for each additional unit produced and sold, while the marginal cost remains constant at $5. Therefore, the company should continue to produce and sell widgets as long as the marginal revenue is greater than or equal to the marginal cost.

In this example, the optimal level of production would be to produce and sell five widgets, where the marginal revenue ($10) is equal to the marginal cost ($5). Any production level beyond this point would result in a decrease in profits, as the marginal cost would exceed the marginal revenue.

Therefore, the marginal revenue and marginal cost approach can help businesses determine the optimal level of production that will maximize profits.

Marginal revenue is the additional revenue that a firm earns from selling one additional unit of output. It can be calculated by subtracting the total revenue of the previous level of output from the total revenue of the current level of output. For example, if a firm sells 10 units of a product for $100 and then sells 11 units for $110, the marginal revenue of the 11th unit is $10.

Marginal cost, on the other hand, is the additional cost that a firm incurs from producing one additional unit of output. It can be calculated by subtracting the total cost of the previous level of output from the total cost of the current level of output. For example, if a firm produces 10 units of a product at a total cost of $80 and then produces 11 units at a total cost of $90, the marginal cost of the 11th unit is $10.

To determine the optimal level of production, a firm should continue to produce as long as the marginal revenue of the next unit of output is greater than or equal to the marginal cost of producing that unit. This is because producing additional units beyond this point will result in decreasing marginal returns, where the additional revenue earned is less than the additional cost incurred.

Therefore, the marginal revenue and marginal cost approach involves comparing the marginal revenue and marginal cost of each additional unit of output in order to determine the profit-maximizing level of production. When the marginal revenue equals the marginal cost, the firm is producing the optimal amount of output where it is earning the most profit possible

23. example of marginal revenue and marginal cost approach​


Answer:

Marginal revenue and marginal cost approach is a technique used to analyze the production processes and pricing strategies of companies. Here is an example of how this approach is used:

Suppose there is a company that produces and sells bicycles. The company wants to determine the optimal price to sell its bicycles and the optimal level of production to maximize profits. To do this, the company can use the marginal revenue and marginal cost approach.

Marginal revenue is the additional revenue generated from selling one more unit of a product. Marginal cost, on the other hand, is the additional cost incurred from producing and selling one more unit of a product.

To find the optimal price and level of production, the company will need to find the point where marginal revenue equals marginal cost.

For example, the company might find that it can produce and sell 100 bicycles at a cost of $50 per unit. If it sells the bicycles for $100 each, the company will earn $10,000 in total revenue. The marginal cost of producing one more bicycle is $50, and the marginal revenue of selling one more bicycle at this price is also $50. At this point, the company is maximizing its profits, as any increase in production beyond 100 bicycles would result in higher marginal costs than marginal revenues.

However, if the company decides to lower the price of bicycles to $80, it might be able to sell more bicycles and generate more revenue. But at what point will the additional revenue from selling more bicycles be countered by the additional cost of producing more bicycles?

Suppose the company decides to sell 150 bicycles at this lower price of $80 per unit. The marginal cost of producing 150 bicycles might increase to $65 per unit due to the need to hire additional workers and incur other expenses. But the marginal revenue of selling each bicycle at $80 remains the same. In this case, the company is still maximizing its profits as marginal revenue is still equal to marginal cost.

By using the marginal revenue and marginal cost approach, the company can determine the optimal price and level of production to maximize profit and make informed decisions about its production and pricing strategies.

How do gross profit margin and operating profit margin differ?

Gross profit margin and operating profit margin are two metrics used to measure a company's profitability. The difference between them is that gross profit margin only figures in the direct costs involved in production, while operating profit margin includes operating expenses like overhead. Both metrics are important in assessing the financial health of a company.

Gross Profit Margin

Gross profit margin shows the percentage of revenue after subtracting the cost of goods sold involved in production. The cost of goods sold is the amount it costs a company to produce the goods or services that it sells. Gross margin shows how well a company generates revenue from the direct costs like direct labor and direct materials involved in producing their products and services.

Gross profit margin is calculated by taking total revenue and subtracting the cost of goods sold. The difference is divided by total revenue. You can multiply the result by 100 since gross margin is typically shown as a percentage.

-

Gross Profit Margin=

Revenue

Revenue−COGS

×100

where:

COGS=Cost of goods sold

Operating Profit MarginOperating profit is located further down the income statement and is derived from its predecessor, gross profit. Operating profit or operating income takes gross profit and subtracts all overhead, administrative, and operational expenses. Operating expenses include rent, utilities, payroll, employee benefits, and insurance premiums. Operating profit includes all operating costs except interest on debt and the company's taxes.Operating profit margin is calculated by taking operating income and dividing it by total revenue. Like gross profit margin, operating profit margin can be expressed as a percentage by multiplying the result by 100 as shown below.
