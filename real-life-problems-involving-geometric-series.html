14. 3 real life word problem in geometric sequence


1. Applying geometric sequences to radioactive decay is a very usual application. It works in the way that, say you start with 100g of element X, then if you have the half-life of say, 1 day, after 1 day, you will be left with 50g. After 2 days, you will have 25g, and so on.

An example problem for this would be: We have 2000g of Element X, which has the half-life of 3 months. When will we run out of Element X?

2. Another example we can use would be the bouncing ball. Usually, a ball bounces high for the first bounce, and then reaches only a part of that for the next bounce, and a part of that for the next as well.

For example: A bouncing ball reaches a height of 60 inches on the first bounce. If on each succeeding bounce, the height is 80% of the previous one, at which bounce will the height be approximately 10 inches?


3. Another usual example is compound interest. Say, for example you deposited 100 today, at an interest rate of 6% per annum. Then at the end of the year you would have 100(1.06). At the end of 2 years, you would have 100(1.06)^2.

For example: You deposit 10,000 in an investment which gives an interest of 3% per annum. How much would you earn from this after 10 years?

27. give 1 real life problem involving geometric sequence and solve ​


Answer:

One of the most common application of geometric series is the calculation of compound interest. Suppose that the interest rate is 5% (wishful thing these day!) and that you invest £100.

After the first year you would have 1.05*£100 = £105.

After the second year you would have (1.05)^2*£100 = £110.25. (Note the extra £0.25 due to the interest being compounded).

After the third year you would have (1.05)^3*£100 = £115.76. (Note the extra £0.76 - actually £0.7625 - due to the interest being compounded).

… and so on … after the nth year, you would have (1.05)^n*£100.
