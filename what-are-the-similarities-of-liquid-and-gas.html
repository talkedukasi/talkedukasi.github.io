Answer:

Explanation:

By tracking seismic waves, scientists have learned what makes up the planet's interior. P-waves slow down at the mantle core boundary, so we know the outer core is less rigid than the mantle. S-waves disappear at the mantle core boundary, so the outer core is liquid. Dense rock layers will allow seismic waves to pass straight through them. Dense interior layers will increase the velocity of seismic waves and change their direction through refraction. Seismic waves from large earthquakes pass throughout the Earth. These waves contain information about the internal structure of the Earth. The velocities and directions of seismic waves reliably differ when they encounter different materials within Earth. What does a reflected wave tell us about Earth's interior? The wave has hit a boundary between two different Earth materials, causing it to reflect back to Earth's surface.
