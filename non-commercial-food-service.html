1. what is non-commercial food service?​


Answer:

Non-commercial food service can be defined as food service operations where food and beverage are not the primary focus of a business, but rather where food and beverage are present to support or supplement a “host.” A variety of labels have been used for this segment over the years, including institutional, non-commercial, contract feeding, on-site food service, and most recently managed services. Organizations can choose to manage food service themselves, which is referred to as self-operated (self-op), or they can contract food service out to a company that specializes in feeding and related services. Three of the dominant players today in non-commercial food service include Aramark, Compass, and Sodexo. While the success of a commercial restaurant is often determined by its sales volume in dollars, non-commercial success is often rated by participation (volume of people). This is especially true in cases where food is free or partially subsidized by the host company for its employees.

11. give three of commercial and three non-commercial catering and explain ​


Answer:

Commercial catering is simply a for-profit business that makes food for parties/weddings/conferences, etc., providing a group of people their meals, for a per person price, that will presumably yield the caterer a profit. That presumption is often erroneous, as a great many catering businesses go belly-up. My father was a lawyer, and he used to say “what most restaurateurs fail to understand is that running a restaurant is not about making delicious food, it’s about having your revenues exceed your costs.”

Non-commercial catering can range from a bunch of Moms getting together to put on a dinner for the boy scout troop, to a church social, to a clambake in support of summer youth programs, to the best cook among your friends saying he’ll put together your 50th birthday dinner.

I ran a clambake 15 times, for about 400 people. We did hope to make a bit of profit, it was a fundraiser of sorts, but I’d organize 15 kids the day before to go to a rocky outcropping across the harbor to pick seaweed and stuff it in burlap bags, then have tables and chairs delivered from the local Taylor rental, get a port-a-potty, then the next day have veggies, lobsters, clams, and beer delivered, build a fire over a bed of rocks, cover it with seaweed, load up racks of food, and cover everything with old army tarps, then have local kids serve everyone dinner, followed by a barndance. Great fun. Non-commercial catering.
