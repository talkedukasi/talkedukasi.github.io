What learnings did I gain from studying phase changes?

I learned that phase changes occur when matter changes from one state to another, such as from a solid to a liquid or from a liquid to a gas.

I also learned that phase changes require a certain amount of energy, either in the form of heat (for melting and vaporization) or in the form of cooling (for freezing and condensation).

What other examples of phase changes can I think of?

One example is sublimation, where a solid directly transforms into a gas without passing through the liquid phase, such as dry ice (solid carbon dioxide) turning into gas when exposed to room temperature.

Another example is deposition, where a gas directly transforms into a solid without passing through the liquid phase, such as frost forming on a cold surface.

What learnings can I share with my family about phase changes?

I can share that phase changes are a natural occurrence in the matter, and that they are a result of the matter absorbing or releasing energy.

I can also share that phase changes can have practical applications in our daily lives, such as in cooking (boiling water to cook pasta) or in air conditioning (cooling air to remove humidity).

Answer:

ACTIVE VOICE

Active voice is a grammatical voice common in many of the world's languages. It is the unmarked voice for clauses featuring a transitive verb in nominative–accusative languages, including English and most other Indo-European languages.

PASSIVE VOICE

A passive voice construction is a grammatical voice construction that is found in many languages. In a clause with passive voice, the grammatical subject expresses the theme or patient of the main verb – that is, the person or thing that undergoes the action or has its state changed.
