11. What are the figures of speech that were used in the poem called my philosophy in life by john ashbery?​


Answer: What are the figures of speech that were used in the poem called my philosophy in life by john ashbery?

"My Philosophy in Life" is a poem by John Ashbery that was published in his collection "Self-Portrait in a Convex Mirror" in 1975. Here is the full text of the poem:

My philosophy in life

is to be as happy as possible

for as long as possible,

provided no one else is made to suffer

too much as a result.

I try to be considerate

of others' feelings,

but I am not too worried

if things don't work out.

I believe that the universe

is essentially benign,

and that the tough times

are merely tests

to see if we are worthy

of the good things

that are bound to come.

I have faith

in the inherent goodness of people,

and I try to live my life

in accordance with this belief.

I try to be kind

and compassionate,

and to always be willing

to lend a helping hand.

I believe that happiness

is a choice,

and that we can choose

to be happy

regardless of our circumstances.

I try to focus on the positive

and to make the best

of every situation.

This, then, is my philosophy in life:

to be as happy as possible

for as long as possible,

while causing as little suffering

to others as I can.

12. Directions: Give a situation that shows the implication of each philosophy in business. Write your answers on a separate sheet of paper. 1. Aristotle's philosophy 2. Plato's philosophy 3. Socrates' philosophy 4. Immanuel Kant's philosophy 5. Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill's philosophy​


Answer:

1.ARISTOTLE PHILOSOPHY

Aristotle combines logic with observation to make general, causal claims. For example, in his biology, Aristotle uses the concept of species to make empirical claims about the functions and behavior of individual animals.

2.PLATO PHILOSOPHY

Plato believes that conflicting interests of different parts of society can be harmonized. The best, rational and righteous, political order, which he proposes, leads to a harmonious unity of society and allows each of its parts to flourish, but not at the expense of others.

3.SOCRATE PHILOSOPHY

He was not interested in mathematics or science but was concerned with the quality of his soul and that of others. Socrates' philosophy examines how we should live. This led him to discussions on various virtues, things like wisdom, justice, courage, piety, and so on.

4.IMMANUEL KANT PHILOSOPHY

Kant argued that the moral law is a truth of reason, and hence that all rational creatures are bound by the same moral law. Thus in answer to the question, “What should I do?” Kant replies that we should act rationally, in accordance with a universal moral law.

5.JEREMY BENTHAM AND JOHN STUART MILL PHILOSOPHY

Bentham and Mill both believed that human actions are motivated entirely by pleasure and pain, and Mill saw that motivation as a basis for the argument that, since happiness is the sole end of human action, the promotion of happiness is the test by which to judge all human conduct.
