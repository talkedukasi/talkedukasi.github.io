14. Learning Tasks 4: What are the Philippine Contemporary Art Forms? Define each in your own words based on the timeline presented. PHILIPPINE ART PERIOD TIMELINE Pre-Colonial Art Period Spanish Colonial Art Period Post War Colonial Art Period American Colonial Art Period Contemporary Art Period ​


Answer:

There are five Philippine contemporary art forms: Pre-Colonial, Spanish Colonial, Post War Colonial, American Colonial, and Contemporary. Each art form has its own unique features and characteristics.

Pre-Colonial art is the earliest form of Philippine art. It is characterized by simple designs and geometric shapes. The most common pre-colonial art forms are pottery and weaving.

Spanish Colonial art is more ornate and elaborate than pre-colonial art. It is often characterized by religious imagery and themes. Common Spanish Colonial art forms include painting, sculpture, and architecture.

Post War Colonial art emerged after the Philippines gained independence from Spain in 1898. This art form is a mix of pre-colonial, Spanish colonial, and American influences. The most common post war colonial art forms are painting and sculpture.

American Colonial art emerged after the United States acquired the Philippines from Spain in 1898. This art form is characterized by realistic depictions of everyday life. Common American Colonial art forms include painting, photography, and architecture.

25. pre colonial period philippine literature summary


Answer:

In Philippine history, the year 1521 marks the arrival of the first colonial power, and the beginning of what is often called the Spanish period. This period is thus described as the pre-colonial era or the pre-Spanish era. The pre-Spanish historical period ending in 1521. Historian Ambeth Ocampo notes an overlap in the history of pre-colonial Philippines and the Spanish colonial period, claiming that while Magellan's arrival in 1521 marked the first documented arrival of European colonizers to this country, it was not until the arrival of Miguel López de Legazpi in 1565 that the Europeans had any marked impact on the lifestyle of the residents of the Philippine Archipelago.

An Indigenous form of communication was already in existence evidenced by writings on barks and bamboos. News were also announced by an umalokohan or the town crier. Literature was already developing: forms included sabi, (maxim), bugtong (riddle), darangan (epic poetry), kumintang (war songs), and hudhud (wedding song).

Spanish colonial motives were not, however, strictly commercial. The Spanish at first viewed the Philippines as a stepping-stone to the riches of the East Indies (Spice Islands), but, even after the Portuguese and Dutch had foreclosed that possibility, the Spanish still maintained their presence in the archipelago. Spanish rule for the first 100 years was exercised in most areas through a type of tax farming imported from the Americas and known as the encomienda. But abusive treatment of the local tribute payers and neglect of religious instruction by encomenderos (collectors of the tribute), as well as frequent withholding of revenues from the crown, caused the Spanish to abandon the system by the end of the 17th century. The governor-general, himself appointed by the king, began to appoint his own civil and military governors to rule directly.

30. write an essay discussing the monetary system of the philippines from the pre-spanish regime to the post-war period​


Answer:

The monetary system of the Philippines has undergone significant changes throughout its history, starting from the pre-Spanish regime up to the post-war period. Prior to the arrival of the Spanish colonizers, the Philippines had a barter system where goods and services were exchanged for other goods and services. The barter system was replaced by the use of gold, silver and copper coins known as piloncitos, which were used by the pre-colonial Filipinos in their trade and economic activities.

When the Spanish colonizers arrived in the Philippines in the 16th century, they introduced the use of the Spanish peso, which became the official currency of the Philippines. The Spanish peso was divided into 8 reales, which was further divided into 100 centavos. The Spanish peso was used for more than 300 years, until the United States of America took control of the Philippines in 1898.

After the Philippines became a colony of the United States, the US dollar was introduced as the official currency. The US dollar was divided into 100 cents, and was used in the Philippines until the end of World War II.

After the war, the Philippines became an independent nation and in 1949, the Philippine peso was re-introduced as the official currency. The Philippine peso was divided into 100 centavos, and was pegged to the US dollar at a rate of 2 pesos to 1 dollar. However, the value of the Philippine peso gradually declined due to economic and political instability, resulting in the government's decision to float the peso in the early 1990s.

Today, the monetary system of the Philippines is based on the Philippine peso, which is divided into 100 centavos. The Central Bank of the Philippines, Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas, is responsible for the issuance, regulation and control of the country's money supply. The monetary system of the Philippines is supported by a robust banking system and a stable economy.

In conclusion, the monetary system of the Philippines has undergone significant changes throughout its history, from the pre-colonial barter system to the use of various currencies such as the Spanish peso, US dollar, and the Philippine peso. The current monetary system is supported by a stable economy and a robust banking system, which helps to maintain economic stability and growth in the country.
