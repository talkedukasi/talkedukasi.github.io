4. What is the Social Stratification system in the Philippines during Pre-Spanish era?​


Answer:

Before the Spanish colonization of the Philippines, the society was organized into social classes or strata based on several factors such as wealth, occupation, and ancestry. At the top of the social hierarchy were the maginoo or nobles, who were wealthy and powerful, and held political and religious positions. The timawa or freemen were the next in rank, followed by the maharlika or warrior class, who were given land grants in exchange for their military service. The alipin or slaves were at the bottom of the social hierarchy, and were either debt slaves or war captives who were forced to work for their masters. Social mobility was limited and usually only occurred through marriage or the acquisition of wealth and power. This social stratification system persisted even after the arrival of the Spanish colonizers and was only gradually dismantled over time.

Pre-Colonial Form of government.

Before the Spaniards came to the Philippines. There were components of Filipino culture that most Filipinos could not make a distinction about, particularly those who had become citizens recently. Prior to the Spanish colonization of the Philippines, the local population of the Philippines lived in communities known as barangays. A barangay was the smallest unit of governance and had between 30 and 100 families. It was separate from the other group and was led by a datu. Several barangays typically usually live close to one another to protect one another in situations of conflict and other emergencies. The occupant of the rank of datu will pass it on to the eldest son or eldest daughter. Later, though, any barangay member could become a chieftain based on his competence and capabilities. He was responsible for overseeing and maintaining the members of his barangay as necessary. In exchange, they were required to pay tribute to the datu, assist him in cropping, and support him in a fight to defend the barangay. When he wished to pass legislation back in the day, a datu had a council of elders to help him out. A town crier known as the umalohokan wrote the rule down and delivered it to the entire barangay.
