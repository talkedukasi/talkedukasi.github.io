2. Reaction mechanism for Biuret test of Casein.​


Answer:

The Biuret test is a common qualitative test for the presence of peptide bonds in a given sample. Casein is a protein found in milk, and it is composed of multiple amino acids linked by peptide bonds. The Biuret test can detect the presence of protein in the sample, and it involves a reaction between copper ions and the peptide bonds.

The Biuret test involves adding a few drops of dilute copper sulfate (CuSO4) solution to the sample solution, followed by the addition of a small amount of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) solution. The addition of sodium hydroxide raises the pH of the solution, causing the peptide bonds in the protein to break down and form complexes with the copper ions. These complexes give a characteristic violet color to the solution, indicating the presence of protein in the sample.

The reaction mechanism for the Biuret test can be summarized as follows:

Step 1: Formation of copper ion complexes

Cu2+ + 2OH- ⇌ Cu(OH)2

Step 2: Formation of peptide bond complexes

Cu(OH)2 + 2R-NH-CO-R' ⇌ [Cu(OH)-(R-NH-CO-R')] + H2O

The final product is a violet-colored complex between the copper ion and the peptide bond, which confirms the presence of protein in the sample. The intensity of the color produced is directly proportional to the amount of protein present in the sample.

In the case of Casein, which is a protein, the peptide bonds present in its structure undergo the same reaction, leading to the formation of violet-colored copper-peptide complexes. This reaction helps in the identification and quantification of Casein protein in a given sample.

6. Which functional group is responsible for the visible positive result in the following tests? a. Biuret test b. Ninhydrin test c. Xantoproteic test d. Millon's test e. Hopkins-Cole test f. Sakaguchi test g. Nitroprusside test h. Fohl's test i. Test for Amide j. Pauly's test


Millon's Test is the functional group that is responsible for the visible positive result.

The development of a red or pink colored precipitate during the Millon's test serves as evidence of a favorable outcome. This suggests that tyrosine or a protein containing tyrosine is present.

Using Millon's test, an analytical technique, one may identify tyrosine, the only amino acid with the phenol group. Millon's test selectively identifies tyrosine, but proteins are not recognized since it also picks up the phenolic group, which can be found in other substances. As a result, additional tests such the Biuret and Ninhydrin tests must be performed in addition to the Millon's test. The test is useful in identifying proteins because tyrosine is a common component of them. The test was developed by French scientist Auguste Nicolas Eugene Millon, who received credit for its creation.

The premise of Millon's test is that complexes with heavy metals like mercury are formed as a result of nitrification of the phenol group in tyrosine. The test ingredient, known as Millon's reagent, is composed of mercuric and mercurous nitrates that have been thoroughly mixed with strong nitric acid. The phenol group on the tyrosine molecule is nitrated by the reagent's nitric acid during the test. The nitrated tyrosine is then mixed with the mercury ions in the solution to produce a red precipitate or solution. Some proteins that include tyrosine react initially with mercuric nitrate to form a white or yellow precipitate.

25. Do you think free amino acids will give a positive result in Biuret test? Why or why not?


Answer:

No.

Explanation:

The biuret test is also used to detect proteins. That is because proteins are made up of polypeptides, which in turn, are made of amino acids joined by peptide bonds. The longer the polypeptide chain is, the more peptide bonds there are, and therefore, the more intense the violet colour will be when biuret test is applied. It also follows that a pale violet or pinkish colour indicates shorter polypeptide chains or fewer peptide bonds. A negative result (lack of violet colour formation) may mean lack of protein, or the presence of free amino acids (without peptide bonds).
