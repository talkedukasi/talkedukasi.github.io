10. If you want to conduct a research study about your favorite restaurant in town what method of qualitative research is appropriate to your study.


Answer:

There are several qualitative research methods that could be appropriate for studying your favorite restaurant in town, depending on the research question, objectives, and the nature of the data you want to collect. Here are a few possibilities:

1. Ethnography: This method involves immersing yourself in the culture and social context of the restaurant to observe and document its daily practices and interactions among staff and customers. You could use participant observation, interviews, and field notes to collect data on topics such as the restaurant's atmosphere, food quality, customer service, and social dynamics.

2. Phenomenology: This method focuses on exploring the subjective experiences of individuals, in this case, customers, who have visited the restaurant. You could conduct in-depth interviews or focus groups with customers to explore their perceptions, opinions, and feelings about the restaurant. This could provide insights into what makes the restaurant appealing or unappealing to customers.

3. Case study: This method involves examining a particular case in depth to gain a detailed understanding of a phenomenon, in this case, the restaurant. You could collect data from various sources, such as the restaurant's website, social media, customer reviews, and interviews with staff and customers. This could provide insights into the restaurant's history, values, operations, and challenges.

Ultimately, the appropriate method would depend on your research question, objectives, and the type of data you want to collect.

26. if there are 3 roads from Town A to Town B and 4 roads from Town B to Town C. How many ways can one go from Town A to C and back to Town A through the Town B without passing through the same road twice?


This problem can be solved using the Fundamental Principle of Counting or FPC. The FPC states that if one event can be done in [tex]m[/tex] ways and another event can be done in [tex]n[/tex] ways, the number of ways that the two events can happen is [tex]mn[/tex] ways. For example, if you have 2 different shirts and 5 different pair of pants, the number of possible combinations of a shirt and a pair pants that you can make is  2×5 = 10 ways.

Now, to solve this problem, we have to remember that the route is this: From A to B, then from B to C, then from C back to B, and from B back to A. 

Let's count our choices:
(a) From A to B, we have 3 roads to choose from;
(b) From B to C, we have 4 roads to choose from;
(c) In going back from C to B, we only have 3 options this time since we already took one in (b), again we cannot take the same road twice;
(d) From B to A, we only have 2 roads to choose from since we already took one in (a).

Therefore, applying the FPC now, we have a total of 3×4×3×2 = 72 ways.

27. 5. If there are 3 roads from Town A to Town B and 4 roads from Town B to Town C, in how many ways can one go from Town A to Town C and back to Town A, through Town B, without passing through the same road twice?​


Answer:This problem involves counting the number of possible routes between three towns, where each route passes through a second town and does not repeat any roads. Let's break it down into steps:

Step 1: Identify the number of roads between each pair of towns. We have 3 roads from Town A to Town B and 4 roads from Town B to Town C.

Step 2: Determine the number of possible routes from Town A to Town C through Town B without passing through the same road twice. To do this, we can use the multiplication principle, which states that if we have m ways of doing one thing and n ways of doing another, then we have m x n ways of doing both things in sequence.

So, we can start by choosing one of the 3 roads from Town A to Town B, then choose one of the 4 roads from Town B to Town C, and finally choose one of the 2 remaining roads from Town C back to Town A. This gives us a total of:

3 x 4 x 2 = 24

possible routes from Town A to Town C and back to Town A, through Town B, without passing through the same road twice.

Therefore, there are 24 possible routes to take.
