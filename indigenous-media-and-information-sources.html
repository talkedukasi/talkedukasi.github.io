15. what is the difference between indigenous media and information sources?​


What is the difference between indigenous media and information sources?

Indigenous media is different from media produced by and for other underserved groups, including racial and sexual minorities, women, and children. For one instance, aboriginal people also lack knowledge about how to engage the media from their village, which is remote from electricity, telephones, the newspapers, or radio or television stations. INDIGENOUS MEDIA AND INFORMATION are talk about communication unique to their people group.

WHAT IS INDIGENOUS?

Native; local; occurring or developed naturally in a specific region/location.

INDIGENOUS KNOWLEDGE

A knowledge that is peculiar to a certain community or society; it is rarely written down.  

INDIGENOUS COMMUNICATION

Transmission of data through local channels or types. It is a method of preserving, passing on, and adapting culture.

INDIGENOUS MEDIA

Types of media expression conceptualized, created, and shared as vehicles for communication by indigenous communities around the world.

The instructor may seek out local examples of how communication is passed down within societies. Identifying the basis of intelligence and the main authorities in a society is critical to this. Local gatherings like kapihan, balitaktakan, and kwentong barber are examples of how news and stories are spread within a community.

Popular media cannot enter certain rural regions. While print, broadcast, and new media have a wide reach, there are still areas where they have not reached.

Indigenous media and information are extremely reliable since they are at the source and are rarely shared for benefit. Because of its direct access to local channels, indigenous media is a vehicle for transformation, education, and development. Ignoring aboriginal media and knowledge will lead to irrelevant and inadequate infrastructure and education programs.

INDIGENOUS MEDIA AND INFORMATION

A local group of people came up with the original information. This often includes content about indigenous peoples that is conveyed by dominant modes of media or by forms of communication that are exclusive to their community group.

21. 1. Why indigenous media is still a practical source of information over other common sources?​


Answer:

Indigenous media may be defined as forms of media expression conceptualized, produced, and circulated by indigenous peoples around the globe as vehicles for communication, including cultural preservation, cultural and artistic expression, political self-determination, and cultural sovereignty. Indigenous media overlap with, and are on a spectrum with, other types of minority-produced media, and quite often they share a kinship regarding many philosophical and political motivations. Indigenous media studies allow us access to the micro-processes of what Roland Robertson has famously called “glocalization”—in this case, the interpenetration of global media technologies with hyperlocal needs, creatively adapted to work within and sustain the local culture rather than to replace it or homogenize it, as some globalization theorists have long feared. The scope of indigenous media studies, a growing field of interdisciplinary scholarship, is quite broad and extensive.
