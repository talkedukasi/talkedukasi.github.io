Explanation:The American Regime (1898-1941)

Filipino revolutionaries have defeated the Spaniards who colonized us for over 300 years. Our flag was hoisted  as a symbol of independence on June 12, 1898. General Emilio Aguinaldo was elected as the first president of the  Republic of the Philippines, but it was short-lived. The Philadelphia Americans were led to defeat by the General. Miguel Malvar in 1903. The peace movement began in his 1900s. Many Filipinos started writing again, and the nationalism of the people remained unwavering.

Filipino writers dealt with all forms of literature: news, reports, poetry, stories, plays, essays and novels. Her writings clearly demonstrated her love for her country and longing for independence.

A lively revival in the field of literature was felt in subsequent newspapers.

El Nuevo Dia (The New Day). In 1900, Sergio Osmea founded the company. Due of Osmea's nationalistic writings, the American censors twice outlawed this and sought to expel him.EL GRITO DEL PUEBLO (The Call of the Nation). Pascual Poblete founded it in 1900.EL RENACIMIENTO (The Rebirth). Rafael Palma established it in 1901. There were also plays created at that time, but the American put a stop to this after the first and second shows because to the persistent nationalistic theme. These were among the items listed:NGAYON, KAHAPON, AND BUKAS (Yesterday, Today and Tomorrow). Written by Aurelio Tolentino, it shows how the Americans suppressed people and intended to dominate the Philippines.Juan Abad's TANIKALANG GINTO.Tomas Remigio's MALAYA.Severino Reyes' WALANG SUGAT.

The Japanese Period (1941–1945)

When the Philippines were once again occupied by a foreign power, this time by Japan, the development of Philippine literature was halted. English-language Philippine literature came to an end.

The progress and growth of Filipino literature in English, and the development of Filipino literature in general, were interrupted during the Japanese period. The Japanese denounced all publications except the Tribune and Philippine Review. During the Japanese era, Filipino literature was phased out in English, with writers writing in Filipino. They did their best to distract them. They rewarded the Filipinos who were loyal to them generously.
