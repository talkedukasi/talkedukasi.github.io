Filipino psychology or Sikolohiyang Pilipino alludes to the psychology conceived out of the experience, thought and direction of the Filipinos, in view of the full utilization of Filipino language and culture.

The methodology is one of ''indigenization from the inside'' by which the methodology and theoretical framework rise out of the encounters of individuals from the native culture. It depends on surveying authentic and socio-social real factors, understanding the nearby language, disentangling Filipino qualities, and clarifying them through the eyes of the local Filipino. Among the results are, an assortment of information including native ideas, advancement of native exploration strategies and native character testing, new headings in showing brain science, and a functioning cooperation in associations among Filipino social scientists and psychologists, both in the overseas and Philippines .

16. Give some of the Filipino ethnocentric behavior and its pros ang cons​


Answer:

Preference for local products: Many Filipinos exhibit a strong preference for locally-made products over foreign ones. This behavior can support local businesses and industries, which can lead to economic growth and employment opportunities. However, it can also lead to limited access to foreign goods, which can hinder innovation and technological advancement.

Strong family ties: Filipinos tend to have strong family ties and prioritize family relationships above all else. This behavior can foster a sense of community and support, providing a safety net for individuals during difficult times. However, it can also result in an insular society that is resistant to change and new ideas.

National pride: Many Filipinos take great pride in their country and its cultural heritage. This behavior can inspire a sense of patriotism and unity, promoting a shared identity among citizens. However, it can also lead to a narrow-minded view of the world, with a lack of appreciation for other cultures and their contributions.

Language preference: Some Filipinos exhibit a preference for speaking the Filipino language over English, especially in formal settings. This behavior can help preserve the Filipino language and culture, promoting national identity and unity. However, it can also hinder communication with other cultures and limit opportunities for international exchange and cooperation.

Ethnocentrism & Cultural Relativism

The two ideas we're going to discuss are ethnocentrism and cultural relativism. During the early days of contact between different cultures, ethnocentrism was the norm. Ethnocentrism is the idea that one's own culture is the main standard by which other cultures may be measured. An ethnocentric is concerned with how similar others' cultural practices, symbols, and beliefs are to their own.

For instance, Ethan is an ethnocentrist; he considers others' beliefs and practices to be savage or corrupt, or he is often confused by other people's cultures. Very often, people that are ethnocentric don't know they are using their culture to judge another's. The culture of an ethnocentric person is considered the 'normal' way that things are done, just as Ethan believes.

A competing idea, cultural relativism is the belief that the culture of people serves particular needs and must be looked at in terms of the world the people inhabit. This is often the perspective of social scientists who work with people and is the result of the work of anthropologist Franz Boas.

For instance, Casey is a cultural relativist; she prefers to look at other cultures in terms of what their practices bring to them. She believes that if a tribe paints their faces for religious ceremonies, there must be a good reason why they do that. Is there a practical reason for it, or is it symbolic? If symbolic, where do the symbols come from? These questions allow a closer examination of the practices of others than ethnocentrism. This doesn't imply that a relativist, like Casey, doesn't have strong beliefs of her own. Rather, other cultures are simply not judged with reference to one's own culture. Again, this often has to be trained into people.
