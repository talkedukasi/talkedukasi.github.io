3. What are the techniques in sociological criticism?


DEFINITION
Sociological criticism is literary criticism directed to understanding (or placing) literature in its larger social context; it codifies the literary strategies that are employed to represent social constructs through a sociological methodology. Sociological criticism analyzes both how the social functions in literature and how literature works in society. This form of literary criticism was introduced by Kenneth Burke, a 20th-century literary and critical theorist, whose article "Literature As Equipment for Living" outlines the specification and significance of such a critique.

TECHNIQUES
Sociological criticism focuses on the relationship between literature and society, the social function of literature. Literature is always produced in a social context. Writers may affirm or criticize the values of the society in which they live, but they write for an audience and that audience is society. 17. why the song of roland is a sociological criticism​


Explanation:

The Song of Roland is a poem that criticizes medieval society by showing the flaws and injustices of the feudal system. It highlights the greed and corruption of the nobles, as well as the suffering and oppression of the common people. Through its characters and events, the poem presents a critique of the social and political structures of the time and raises questions about the morality and fairness of these systems. By doing so, it serves as a sociological criticism of medieval society.

26. EXPLAIN THE PHENOMENA OF FESTIVAL USING THE STRUCTURAL FUNCTIONALISM, INTERPRETIVE AND CRITICAL PERSPECTIVE OF SOCIOLOGY. ​.


Answer:

Structural Functionalism: Festivals are an important part of social cohesion, as they bring people together to celebrate a common culture and values. They provide a sense of shared identity, a sense of belonging, and contribute to social stability. The various activities that take place during a festival encourage friendship and mutual understanding, strengthen trust and create a sense of unity.

Interpretive Perspective: Festivals are a way for people to express themselves and their values. They can be used as a means of shedding traditional status hierarchies and embracing creativity, new ideas, and cultural exchange. The performance, display, and discussion of various art forms can provide a way to challenge existing norms and form new collective understandings.

Critical Perspective: Festivals can act as a form of societal control by reinforcing dominant values and suppressing minority perspectives. They can be used to celebrate a certain power structure or a particular version of history as well as to discredit cultural practices seen as inappropriate or politically unpopular. These events can be manipulated and used as a tool by the powerful to maintain their own interests.

Answer:

Festivals are a crucial component of social cohesiveness because they bring people together to celebrate a shared culture and set of values, according to structural functionalism. They promote social stability by fostering a sense of shared identity and belonging. A festival's numerous events foster unity, trust, and a sense of community while fostering friendship and intercultural understanding.

Interpretation: People can express who they are and what they believe in via festivals. They can be applied as a strategy for letting go of conventional status hierarchies and embracing innovation, fresh thinking, and cross-cultural communication. Different art forms can offer an opportunity to question social conventions and create new, shared understandings through performance, display, and discussion.
