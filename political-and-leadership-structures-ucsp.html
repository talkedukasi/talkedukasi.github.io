7. understanding culture society and politics or ucsp strand abm


About Understanding Culture, Society, and Politics

As part of the K to 12 program implemented by the Department of Education in the Philippines, UCSP or Understanding Culture, Society, and Politics is part of the basic education curriculum. It can either be taken during the 11th grade or in the 12th grade. It is an eighty-hour subject to be taken for one semester.  

This course is related to the insights of Anthropology, Political Science, and Sociology. All strands under the Senior High School level is mandated to undergo this subject. It develops the awareness and knowledge of the learners with regards to the political dynamics, cultural diversity, and social sensitivity.

14. human social change and political identities (ucsp)​


Human social change and political identities (UCSP) is a field of study that examines how social, political, economic, and cultural changes impact individual and collective identities, and how these identities shape political actions and institutions. This field is interdisciplinary, drawing on insights from sociology, anthropology, political science, and other social sciences.

UCSP scholars analyze how various forms of social change, such as globalization, economic restructuring, technological innovation, and demographic shifts, shape the identities of individuals and groups. They also investigate how identity formation influences political behavior, such as voting, protesting, and engaging in social movements.

UCSP also involves examining how political institutions, such as governments and political parties, interact with identity-based groups and movements. Scholars in this field explore how political institutions can either facilitate or hinder the ability of marginalized groups to achieve their goals, such as gaining representation or advancing social justice.

Overall, UCSP provides a framework for understanding the complex relationship between social change, political identities, and institutions, and how these factors shape the course of political and social movements.

Abstract

Change in political leadership can directly affect climate change mitigation policies. This is particularly true in sectors such as transport, where often long-term investments by individuals (e.g., in cars) and by local and national governments (e.g., transport infrastructure and services) are made. The large potential for efficient solutions to reduce CO2 emissions and to improve the sustainability of the transport sector has been so far unexploited. It is hard to understand why this potential has not yet been exploited, considering its cost-effectiveness and the potential for cobenefits. Particularly interesting is the fact that countries with relatively similar economic performances and similar access to efficient technologies and vehicles show substantial differences in the development of their transport CO2 emissions over the past 30 years. This study applies some well-established political science theories on the example of climate change mitigation in the transport sector in order to identify some of the factors that could help explain these differences in the success of policies and strategies. The analysis suggests that institutional set-ups that contribute to consensus building in the political process provide the political and policy stability that is necessary for changes in sectors that rely on long-term investments. However, there is no direct correlation between institutional structures, e.g., corporatism and success in reducing greenhouse gas emissions in the transport sector. Before actual policy progress can be observed, environmental objectives need to be built into the consensus-based policy structure.
