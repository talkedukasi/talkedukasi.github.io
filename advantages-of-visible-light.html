The Definition of Visible Light

Visible light is the range of electromagnetic radiation that can be detected by the human eye. The wavelength associated with this range is 380 to 750 nanometers (nm), while the frequency range is about 430 to 750 terahertz (THz).

The visible spectrum is the part of the electromagnetic spectrum between infrared and ultraviolet. Infrared radiation, microwaves, and radio waves have a lower frequency and wavelength than visible light, while ultraviolet light, x-radiation, and gamma radiation have a shorter frequency and wavelength than visible light.

 

Visible Light Properties

Visible light, which is more commonly referred to as "light", is the part of the electromagnetic spectrum that can be seen by the human eye.

In the electromagnetic spectrum, visible light lies between infrared and ultraviolet, which is 380–750 nm or 430–750 THz. The size is taken based on the light that can be observed by humans. However, age and other factors can affect this range, as some people can see both infrared and ultraviolet light.

The visible spectrum is roughly divided into colors, which are usually called red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, and purple. However, these divisions are not equal in size and are not very accurate.

The study of visible light and its interaction with matter is called optics.

 

Color of visible light

The colors of visible light are called the visible spectrum. The colors of the spectrum correspond to a range of wavelengths.

Sir Isaac Newton divided the spectrum into red, orange, yellow, green, blue, and violet. He later added indigo, but Newton's "indigo" was closer to modern "blue", while his "blue" was more like modern "cyan".

Color names and wavelength ranges are somewhat arbitrary, but they follow the order from infrared to ultraviolet: infrared, red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, violet, and ultraviolet.

Modern scientists refer to colors by wavelength rather than by name, to avoid confusion.

21. Advantages and disadvantages of infrared, visible light, ultra Violet rays,x rays​


Infrared:

Advantages:

1. Can penetrate some materials like smoke, fog, and dust that visible light cannot, making it useful in imaging and detection applications.

2. Can be used for remote temperature sensing and measurement.

3. Can be used in communication technology, such as in remote controls and data transfer between devices.

Disadvantages:

1. Can cause skin burns and eye damage at high intensities.

2. Can interfere with some electronic devices, such as remote controls or security sensors.

3. Can be absorbed by some materials, making it difficult to penetrate certain objects.

Visible Light:

Advantages:

1. Can be seen by the human eye, allowing for visual communication and interpretation.

2. Can be used in photography and videography to capture images and videos.

3. Can be used in lighting applications to illuminate spaces and objects.

Disadvantages:

1. Can be easily blocked or reflected by certain materials, limiting its use in some applications.

2. Can cause eye damage at high intensities, such as from staring at the sun.

3. Can be absorbed by certain materials, altering their appearance or rendering them invisible.

Ultra Violet (UV) Rays:

Advantages:

1. Can be used in medical applications, such as for germicidal treatment or phototherapy.

2. Can be used in forensic applications, such as detecting blood or other bodily fluids.

3. Can be used in some industrial processes, such as curing coatings or adhesives.

Disadvantages:

1. Can cause skin and eye damage, including sunburns and increased risk of skin cancer.

2. Can be absorbed by some materials, making it difficult to penetrate certain objects.

3. Can be harmful to plant and animal life, including marine ecosystems.

X-rays:

Advantages:

1. Can penetrate solid objects, allowing for imaging and inspection of internal structures.

2. Can be used in medical applications, such as for diagnostic imaging or cancer treatment.

3. Can be used in some industrial applications, such as inspecting welds or detecting flaws in materials.

Disadvantages:

1. Can cause radiation exposure, which can lead to DNA damage, cancer, or other health issues.

2. Can be harmful to developing fetuses and children, as their cells are more vulnerable to radiation damage.

3. Can be expensive and require specialized equipment and training to use safely.
