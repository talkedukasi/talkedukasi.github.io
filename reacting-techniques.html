1. Combustion Reaction

Combustion reactions are reactions with combustible materials with oxidizing agents to produce oxidized products. Oxidizers are chemicals that fuels need to burn, generally oxygen. Consider the example of burning magnesium metal.

2Mg + O2 → 2MgO + Heat

Here, 2 magnesium atoms react with oxygen molecules to produce 2 molecules of magnesium oxide compound which releases heat in the process.

2. Combination Reaction

A combination reaction is a chemical reaction in which two or more reactants combine to form a single product is known as a combination reaction. The form of the equation is as below:

X + Y → XY

Combination reactions are also known as synthesis reactions.

Combination reaction example: 2Na + Cl2 → 2NaCl

3. Decomposition Reaction

Reactions in which one compound breaks down into two or more simpler compounds are known as decomposition reactions. The form of the equation is as below: XY → X + Y

Reactions in which compounds decompose upon heating are known as thermal decomposition reactions. Decomposition reactions are the opposite of combination reactions. An example of a decomposition reaction: CaCO3 → CaO + CO2

4. Displ4cement Reaction

A chemical reaction in which a more reactive element displ4ces a less reactive element from a salt solution. The form of the equation is

X + YZ → XZ + Y

Displ4cement reactions are also called substitution reactions. An example of a displ4cement reaction: Zn + CuSO4 → ZnSO4 + Cu

5. Double Displ4cement Reaction

A double displ4cement reaction is a chemical reaction that occurs in which ions are exchanged between two reactants forming a new compound.

Chemical reactions involving the formation of insoluble products (precipitates; solids) are called precipitation reactions. The reactants are soluble, but the products formed are insoluble and separ4te as solids.

13. Hydrocarbons are often made to react with hydrogen gas (H2) in petroleum refining. If pentene, C5H10, is reacted with hydrogen to form pentane, C5H12, how many moles of hydrogen are needed to react with 487 moles of pentene?​


Solution:

Step 1: Write the balanced chemical equation.

C₅H₁₀ + H₂ → C₅H₁₂

Step 2: Determine the mole ratio needed.

mole ratio = 1 mol H₂ : 1 mol C₅H₁₀

Step 3: Calculate the number of moles of H₂ needed using the mole ratio.

[tex]\text{moles of H₂ = 487 mol C₅H₁₀} × \frac{\text{1 mol H₂}}{\text{1 mol C₅H₁₀}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{moles of H₂ = 487 mol}}[/tex]
