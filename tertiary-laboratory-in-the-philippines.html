10. PHILIPPINE EDUCATION SYSTEM -- HISTORY --PRIMARY EDUCATION --SECONDARY EDUCATION --TERTIARY EDUCATION​


Answer:

The Philippine education system has a long and unique history, beginning with the primary, secondary, and tertiary levels. From early colonization to the present day, numerous factors have influenced the development of education in the country. At the primary level, compulsory education was introduced by Spanish colonizers in 1863. This laid the foundation for a system designed to create literate citizens, who could then progress to higher levels of education. Secondary schooling soon followed with technical and vocational programs being offered as well as academic curricula. This led to increased enrolment in universities and colleges across the nation at the tertiary level, where students can further specialize their studies and pursue professional careers.The Philippine education system is divided into three parts: primary, secondary, and tertiary education.

Primary education in the Philippines starts at the age of six and lasts for six years. Students spend three years in elementary school and three years in junior high school. During this time, they learn basic academic subjects such as math, science, English, and Filipino.

Secondary education in the Philippines begins at the age of 12 and lasts for four years. Students spend two years in high school and two years in senior high school. During this time, they focus on more specialized academic subjects and prepare for college entrance exams.

Tertiary education in the Philippines typically begins at the age of 18 and lasts for four years. Students can either attend a university or a vocational/technical school. At a university, they can choose to study a specific academic discipline or pursue a general course of study. Vocational/technical schools provide training in specific trades such as carpentry, plumbing, or welding.

21. distinct primary to secondary from tertiary sources in philippine history. ​


Answer:

In Philippine history, primary sources are original materials that were created during the time period being studied. These include documents such as letters, diaries, government records, and photographs. Examples of primary sources in Philippine history include the Philippine Declaration of Independence in 1898, the Malolos Constitution of 1899, and the Philippine-American Treaty of Paris in 1898.

Secondary sources, on the other hand, are materials that were created after the time period being studied and are based on primary sources. These include books, articles, and documentaries that analyze and interpret primary sources. Examples of secondary sources in Philippine history include textbooks, historical monographs, and scholarly articles on the Philippine Revolution or the Philippine-American War.

Tertiary sources are materials that summarize or provide an overview of secondary sources. These include encyclopedias, dictionaries, and other reference materials. Examples of tertiary sources in Philippine history include history textbooks, historical atlases, and online historical databases.

It's worth noting that these categorization of sources can be subjective and context-dependent, it also varies depending on the field of study.

30. why do we need to trace the development of scientific crime laboratories in the philippines?


Answer:

In June of 1972, the Crime Laboratory was established through the directive issued by National Police Commission Chairman CRISPIN M DE CASTRO.

Training courses in various fields of Forensic Science were offered to students and would-be investigators of the Philippine Constabulary/Integrated National Police.

On August 1975, pursuant to Presidential Decree 765 and the Headquarters PC/INP Staff Memorandum Number 20 dated 08 June 1976, personnel of the Crime Laboratory of the Academic Division of the Integrated National Police Academy were placed under the responsibility of the Integrated National Police Training Center.

On October 1991, the Crime Laboratory was transferred to Camp Mariano Castañeda in Silang, Cavite pursuant to RA 6975. On 01 October 1993, it was revitalized and served as a Training Division of the National Police College. On 03 November of the following year, DILG Circular Nr 93-28 signed by SECRETARY RAFAEL M ALUNAN III caused the activation and transfer of the Educational and Training Center under the control and supervision of the Philippine Public Safety College.

DIRECTOR EUGENIO C. CRUZ, JR. became the third Director and first civilian head of NCRTI during the transition years from 1994 to 1998. POLICE SUPERINTENDENT TEODORO S CRUZ took command for seven months in 1999. And POLICE SUPERINTENDENT ANDRES Z AGSALDA held the post for four months between 1999, and 2000.
