6. No ethnic art form survived after the colonizers arrived in the Philippines(true or false) ​


Answer:

False.

Explanation:

Many ethnic art forms survived the colonization of the Philippines. Examples of which are the following:

Ethnomedicine - the use of traditional medicine such as hilot and herbs that were passed down by the babaylansTextile, carving, weaving, pottery, and other material culture Oral literature - epics, myths, songs, and even superstitious beliefsDance, music, and other theatrical artsLiterature

These are some of the examples of ethnic art forms in the Philippines that persisted up until the present. While colonialization destroyed many of the traditional records, such as those written in Baybayin, these non-perishable artefact of early Filipinos survived up until today.

17. artsworks of ethnic Arts​


Philippines Ethnic Art and Crafts

Ethnic arts in Philippines are labors of love and patience. The intricate carvings on wood, metal, stone or glass are products of skillful hands and imaginative minds.

Indigenous musical instruments like the flute, drums, rainsticks, native guitars and bells are made of bamboo, twigs, pebbles, animal skin, seeds and seashells. The careful manipulation of these materials magically produces sounds and unique instruments used in tribal dances and ceremonies.

(The tumpong (also inci by the Maranao) is a type of Philippine bamboo flute used by the Maguindanaon, half the size of the largest bamboo flute, the palendag.)

(Sulibaw - a hollow Igorot drum, topped with pig skin or lizard skin.)

(Rain Sticks - simulate the sound of rainfall, and were originally used in native ceremonies during dry months to ask the gods for rain.)

(Karaga - is a guitar made up of bamboo where the strings are slit from the bamboo itself.)

(Tongkaling brass bells, used as an amulet or as a musical instrument.)


18. what particular aspect of Philippine culture identity do local arts reveal? how do local arts enliven diversity of ethnic arts? ​


Answer:

Filipino art reflects a society with diverse cultural backgrounds.

The Malays first came in contact with traders who brought Chinese and Indian customs. The Malays in the southern Philippine Islands were first introduced to Muslim culture in the 14th century.

Under Spanish rule and, later, in the United States, many modern aspects of Philippine culture emerged. The Spaniards introduced a foreign culture based on Catholicism in the 16th century. Although religious fervor was growing in the lowlands, Muslims and some northern tribes kept their cultural differences.

A class of intellectuals emerged from among the enthusiasts, who began to develop the Philippine literary culture of the day. The Philippines became one of the most Westwestern countries in Southeast Asia during the first half of the twentieth century, thanks to American influence.

The cultural movement in Europe and the United States had a profound effect on Filipino artists, even after independence in 1946. However, although Western forms are drawn, the works of Filipino artists, writers, and artists are full of vivid Filipino themes. By highlighting the richness of the archipelago in all its diversity, Filipino artists have helped to shape the sense of nationalism.

Many Malay customs remain intact despite centuries of foreign domination. Muslims and tribal groups maintain a diverse culture in music, dance, and carpentry. In addition, many Filipino artists combine traditional images into modern genres.

23. Philippine ethnicity


Ethnic groups in the Philippines:
The Philippines is inhabited by more than 175 ethnolinguistic nations, the majority of whose languages are Austronesian in origin. Many of these nations converted to Christianity, particularly the lowland-coastal nations, and adopted foreign elements of culture. Ethnolinguistic nations include the Ilocano, Ivatan, Pangasinan, Kapampangan, Tagalog, Bicolano, Visayans (Aklanon, Boholano, Butuanon, Capiznon, Cebuano, Cuyonon, Eskaya, Hiligaynon, Karay-a, Masbateño, Porohanon, Romblomanon, Suludnon, Surigaonon and Waray-Waray), Zamboangueño, Subanon, and more.
Dominant ethnic groups by province.
In western Mindanao and the Sulu Archipelago, there are ethnolinguistic nations who practice Islam. The Spanish called them Moros after the Moors, despite no resemblance or cultural ties to them apart from their religion. In the Agusan Marsh and the highlands of Mindanao, there are native ethnic groups collectively known as the Lumad. Most maintain their animistic beliefs and traditions, though some of them have converted to Christianity as well.
The Negrito were among the earliest humans to settle the Philippines. The first known were the people of the Tabon man remains. The Negrito population was estimated in 2004 at around 31,000.[1] Their tribal groups include the Ati, and the Aeta. Their ways of life remain mostly free from Western and Islamic influences. Scholars study them to try to understand pre-Hispanic culture.
Traditional homelands of the indigenous peoples of the Philippines
Overview of the spread & overlap of languages spoken throughout the country
Other ethnolinguistic nations form a minority in the Philippine population. These include those of Japanese,[2] Chinese (particularly the Hokkien or Fujianese), Indians[3] (particularly the Punjabi, Tamil and Keralites), Malay, and those of Spanish descent.
