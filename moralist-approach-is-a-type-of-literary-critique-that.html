2. In 5 sentences each, describe the following Literary Approaches to Critiquing a Literary Piece.1. Independent Literary Critique2. Formalist Approach3. Structuralist Approach4. Moralist Approach5. Historical Approach​


Independent Literary Critique involves an objective analysis of a literary piece based on the critic's personal interpretation of the text, rather than any specific theoretical approach or framework.

The Formalist Approach emphasizes the formal elements of a literary text, such as language, structure, and style, to analyze the work as an autonomous entity without reference to external factors or historical context.

The Structuralist Approach focuses on the underlying structures and patterns of a literary text, examining the relationships between characters, symbols, and themes to uncover the deeper meaning of the work.

The Moralist Approach evaluates a literary piece based on its moral or ethical implications, examining how the work reflects the values and beliefs of the author and society at large.

The Historical Approach considers the historical context in which a literary piece was written and the social and cultural forces that influenced its creation, examining the work as a product of its time and place in history.

12. Critique the poem below by writing a 2-paragraph essay andremember to use the moralist approach.


MORALIST APPROACH (CRITIQUING A POEM)

Robert Frost's poem "The Road Not Taken" is a well-known and widely read work of literature. Readers and academics alike have interpreted it in a variety of ways, but some have condemned it for encouraging independence and selfishness. It is crucial to examine the poem's meaning and evaluate whether it encourages moral action using the moralist method.

The poem tells the story of a person standing at a fork in the road and pondering which path to take. In the end, the narrator chooses the less-traveled road, which he claims has made all the difference in his life. On the surface, this message may seem admirable - the importance of taking risks and making your own choices. However, a closer look reveals a more selfish perspective.

The narrator chose the less-traveled road because it was unique and made him feel special, not because it was the best choice for him or for others. This promotes individualism over consideration for others and the common good. In addition, the final line "I took the one less traveled by, / And that has made all the difference" implies that the narrator believes he made the better choice, suggesting a sense of moral superiority. This type of thinking can lead to a lack of empathy and understanding for others, which is not a virtuous quality.

In conclusion, while "The Road Not Taken" by Robert Frost may seem like an inspiring and uplifting poem, a moralist analysis reveals potential flaws in its message. By promoting individualism and selfishness, it can lead to a lack of concern for others and a sense of moral superiority. It is important to recognize and address these issues in literature and in our daily lives to promote virtuous behavior and consider the common good.

30. LITERARY CRITICISM : (FORMALIST APPROACH) list five ideas about moralist approach​


Answer:

1. The moralist approach is a philosophical view that emphasizes the importance of morality and ethical behavior in human life and decision-making.

2. According to the moralist approach, there are objective moral standards that apply universally to all individuals and societies, and it is our duty to abide by these standards.

3. The moralist approach often involves a focus on virtues, such as honesty, compassion, and fairness, and the cultivation of these virtues through education and practice.

4. The moralist approach can be contrasted with relativist or subjectivist views, which hold that moral standards are relative to individual beliefs, cultural norms, or personal preferences.

5. While the moralist approach can be a valuable guide for ethical decision-making, critics argue that it can be too rigid and inflexible, and may not take into account the complexities and nuances of real-world situations.
