11. Discuss the importance of microorganism in the environment and how these organisms help maintain the ecological balance of the ecosystem. Provide an example.


Microorganisms play a vital role in the environment. Microorganisms such as bacteria, fungi and protozoans are what we call decomposers. They decompose the tissues of dead organisms to get their food, in a way they give back nutrients into the soil which would later on be absorbed by producers which provides us food. Example

When an animal dies, bacteria serves as a decomposer which decomposes the corpse. They recycle nutrients that are mixed with the soil. The plants absorb them which gives us food. Without microorganisms the food chain would be broken and we won't have food to eat.

Microorganism act as decomposers in the ecosystem which returns some of the nutrients like nitrogen to be available again in the ecosystem. Without decomposers, some nutrients will not recycle and available again in the ecosystem. Some microorganism also helps convert substances to usable substance for other living organism.

Example: In nitrogen cycle,

microorganism like Nitrogen fixing bacteria, nitrifying bacteria, and denitrifying bacteria helps in recycling the nitrogen nutrients to be available for plants.

27. how do the seven principle of environment and environmental laws help to maintain ecological balance.​


Seven principles of environmental law help maintain ecological balance

1. Duty To Prevent Reduce And Control Environmental Harm

International law obliges each country to take the necessary steps to control and deal with sources of serious global pollution or sources of transboundary destruction that are within their jurisdiction.

2. Due diligence and harm prevention

Principal due diligence determines that every good government should socialize administrative law provisions governing public and private actions in order to protect the state, others and the global environment. The advantage of this standard is its flexibility, and the state is not the only guarantor for harm prevention.

This principle will be applied by considering all aspects of a government, whether in terms of effective or not regional control, available natural resources, as well as the nature of the activities carried out. However, the disadvantage is that it becomes unclear the provisions regarding the form of regulation and control requested from each country, because it depends on the conditions of the country concerned.

3. Absolute Obligation Of Prevention

This provision requires each country to make every effort to prevent pollution from occurring, and that the state is responsible for environmental damage that is unavoidable or unforeseen. However, this principle is considered to go too far to limit the freedom of the state in determining policies regarding the environment in its own territory.

This principle also only emphasizes the obligation of proof and responsibility for damage to the party causing the pollution, rather than emphasizing proper supervision.

4. Foreseeability of harm and the “pre-cautionary principle”

Based on this principle, the state is required to calculate each of its policies relating to the environment. The state is obligated to prevent or prohibit actions that previously could be expected to cause environmental damage.

5.Transboundary Co-operation In Causes Of Environmental Risk

The second principle in environmental law is that every country must cooperate with other countries, in terms of handling cross-border pollution. This is in line with the recognition that there are times when the country has "Shared Natural Resources" which must be utilized together. This principle was outlined in the 1972 Stockholm Declaration.

6. The "Polluters Pays" Principle

This principle places more emphasis on the economic aspect than the legal aspect, because it regulates the wisdom of calculating the value of damages and their imposition.

7. Equal Access And Non-Discrimination

The basic provisions of this principle are that foreign parties can also use the compensation provisions that exist in the national law of a country regarding cross-border pollution caused by the country concerned. This principle must be applied equally without any discriminatory action. This principle requires equal treatment to both national legal subjects and foreign legal subjects without any differences.
