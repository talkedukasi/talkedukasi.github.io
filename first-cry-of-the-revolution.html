10. Which among the three accounts of the first cry of the revolution became prominent? ​


Answer:

At the close of August 1896, members of the Katipunan secret society (Katipuneros) led by Andrés Bonifacio rose up in revolt somewhere in an area referred to as Caloocan,[2] wider than the jurisdiction of present-day Caloocan City which may have overlapped into present-day Quezon City.[3]

Originally the term cry referred to the first clash between the Katipuneros and the Civil Guards (Guardia Civil). The cry could also refer to the tearing up of community tax certificates (cédulas personales) in defiance of their allegiance to Spain. The inscriptions of "Viva la Independencia Filipina" can also be referred as term for the cry. This was literally accompanied by patriotic shouts.[4]

Because of competing accounts and ambiguity of the place where this event took place, the exact date and place of the Cry is in contention.[3][4] From 1908 until 1963, the official stance was that the cry occurred on August 26 in Balintawak. In 1963 the Philippine government declared a shift to August 23 in Pugad Lawin, Quezon City.[4]

At the close of August 1896, members of the Katipunan secret society (Katipuneros) led by Andrés Bonifacio rose up in revolt somewhere in an area referred to as Caloocan,[2] wider than the jurisdiction of present-day Caloocan City which may have overlapped into present-day Quezon City.[3]

Originally the term cry referred to the first clash between the Katipuneros and the Civil Guards (Guardia Civil). The cry could also refer to the tearing up of community tax certificates (cédulas personales) in defiance of their allegiance to Spain. The inscriptions of "Viva la Independencia Filipina" can also be referred as term for the cry. This was literally accompanied by patriotic shouts.[4]

Because of competing accounts and ambiguity of the place where this event took place, the exact date and place of the Cry is in contention.[3][4] From 1908 until 1963, the official stance was that the cry occurred on August 26 in Balintawak. In 1963 the Philippine government declared a shift to August 23 in Pugad Lawin, Quezon City.[4]

[tex]\huge\color{purple}{\boxed{{\colorbox{black}{ANSWER}}}}[/tex]

The First Cry of the Revolution or "Cry of Pugad Lawin" refers to the event that marked the start of the Philippine Revolution against Spanish colonial rule. However, there are conflicting views about this historical event, particularly in terms of its significance and who should be credited for it. Here are some of the conflicting views:

Significance - Some people argue that the Cry of Pugad Lawin was the actual beginning of the revolution, while others believe that it was just one of many factors that contributed to the eventual uprising. Some also argue that other events, such as the execution of Jose Rizal and the propaganda movement, were more significant in sparking the revolution.

Crediting the leaders - There is also debate about who should be credited for the Cry of Pugad Lawin. While it is commonly attributed to Andres Bonifacio and the Katipunan, some historians argue that it was actually initiated by Melchora Aquino and her group of followers. Others also argue that the Katipunan was not yet well-organized at the time, and that the cry was a spontaneous and uncoordinated act of rebellion.

Role of the masses - Another conflicting view is the role of the masses in the revolution. Some argue that the Cry of Pugad Lawin was a result of the common people's pent-up frustrations and desire for freedom, while others believe that it was the result of a small group of educated elites who were disillusioned with Spanish colonial rule.

Overall, the conflicting views about the First Cry of the Revolution reflect the complexities and nuances of Philippine history, and highlight the need for ongoing research, discussion, and critical analysis of our past.
