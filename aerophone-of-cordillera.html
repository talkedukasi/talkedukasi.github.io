Explanation:

Idiophone

-is any musical instrument that creates sound primarily by the vibration of the instrument itself, without the use of air flow, strings or membranes.

Chordophone

-is a musical instrument that makes sound by way of a vibrating string or strings stretched between two points. It is a stringed instrument.

Aerophone

-is a musical instrument that produces sound primarily by causing a body of air to vibrate. It is a a wind instrument.

Membranophone

-is a musical instruments whose sound is generated by striking, rubbing, or singing into a stretched membrane.

Gangsa

Gangsa is a single smooth-surfaced gong with a narrow rim that is handled in one hand. A gangsa set consists of gangsa tuned to different notes, based on regional or local cultural preferences, and is played one gong per player. The amount of gangsa in a set varies according to availability and the customs of a certain ethnic group in the Luzon Cordillera: Kalinga, Ifugao, Bontoc, and so on.

Kalaleng or Tongali

Because the kalaleng is long and has a small internal diameter, it is able to play different harmonics by overblowing, even with only one nostril's airflow. As a result, this nose flute has a range of two and a half octaves.

Diwdiw-as

A rattan thread ties a bunch of saggeypo from numerous nodes of bamboo of varying lengths in a row. Blowing over the open holes is how it's done. They are constructed from the same narrow part of the bamboo as saggeypo.

Saggeypo

It's a bamboo pipe with a node on one end and an open end pressed against the player's lower lip while he blows directly over the top. The pipe can be played by one person alone or in groups of three or more.

Pateteg

Patatag is named after "pateteg," an Ilocano term for one of the Philippines' indigenous musical instruments, the bamboo xylophone, which is popular among the Kalinga of Mt. Province.

Paldong

It's a single-end-blown open flute. Three finger holes are located on the flute's lower end. The instrument is composed of bamboo, with the upper edge chopped away obliquely from the back and somewhat from the front. Both ends of the paldong are open, and there are four fingerholes in total: three in front and one in the rear. The lower lip of the player is pressed against the cutaway surface.
