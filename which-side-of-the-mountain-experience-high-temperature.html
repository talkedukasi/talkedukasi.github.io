Answer:

1. As water vapor rises over a mountain, it cools and condenses into liquid droplets. This process is known as orographic lifting. As the air rises, it expands and cools, causing the water vapor to condense into clouds or fog. The clouds or fog then release precipitation in the form of rain or snow on the windward side of the mountain

2. The side of the mountain that receives the most direct sunlight will experience higher temperatures

3. When air becomes warmer and drier as it moves down the leeward side, it is known as a process called subsidence. This process causes the air to sink, which in turn reduces the amount of moisture in the air and increases its temperature. This can lead to increased temperatures and decreased humidity levels on the leeward side of mountains or other landforms.

5. The back of a mountain can form a variety of landforms, including valleys, ridges, cliffs, and plateaus. Valleys are formed when two mountains erode away at their base, creating a trough-like depression. Ridges are formed when two mountains erode away at their sides, creating a sharp peak. Cliffs are formed when the sides of a mountain erode away, leaving steep walls of rock. Plateaus are formed when the top of a mountain erode away, leaving flat land at the top.

6. Topography affects climate by influencing the amount of sunlight and heat that an area receives, the amount of moisture in the air, and the circulation of air masses. For example, areas with higher elevations tend to be cooler than areas at lower elevations due to decreased atmospheric pressure. Mountain ranges can also act as barriers to air masses, creating distinct climates on either side. Additionally, topography can affect wind patterns and precipitation levels.

1.) What happens to water vapor as it rises over the mountain?

As water vapor rises over the mountain, it cools down and condenses into clouds. This process can produce precipitation on the windward side of the mountain.

2.) Which side of the mountain experiences low temperature?

The windward side of the mountain experiences low temperature due to the cooling effect of the rising air.

3.) Which side of the mountain experiences high temperature?

The leeward side of the mountain experiences high temperature due to the warming effect of the descending dry air.

4.) Which side of the mountain would have lush or thick vegetation (more plants)? Explain.

The windward side of the mountain is more likely to have lush or thick vegetation (more plants) due to the availability of moisture from the precipitation produced by the rising air. The leeward side, on the other hand, is more likely to have a drier and less vegetated environment due to the descending dry air.

5.) What happens when air becomes warmer and drier as it moves down the leeward side?

When air becomes warmer and drier as it moves down the leeward side, it can evaporate moisture from the ground and vegetation, leading to arid conditions and desertification in some cases.

6.) What dry region forms at the back of the mountain?

A dry region called the rain shadow forms at the back of the mountain. This is caused by the descending dry air that has already lost most of its moisture on the windward side.

7.) How does topography affect climate?

Topography affects climate by influencing the movement of air masses, creating barriers that can cause precipitation or drought, and creating microclimates that can differ from surrounding regions.

8.) Describe the climate of the areas on both sided of the Sierra Madre.

The Sierra Madre Mountain Range runs north to south on the eastern portion of Luzon in the Philippines. The windward side of the mountain range, facing the Pacific Ocean, experiences high precipitation and cooler temperatures, while the leeward side experiences drier conditions and higher temperatures. The areas on the windward side are more likely to have lush vegetation, while the leeward side is more likely to have a drier and less vegetated environment. The climate of the areas on both sides of the Sierra Madre is heavily influenced by the barrier effect of the mountain range, which reduces the strength of incoming typhoons from the Pacific Ocean before reaching the central mainland.
