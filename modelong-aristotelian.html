Answer:

The differences are easy enough to enumerate: helio-centric v. geo-centric solar system, and so on. The similarities are less obvious.

They are similar, though, in that each expressed the most advanced theory of physics that could be known in their day. Perhaps if Aristotle had been smarter, he might have come up with some of the physical experiments that Galileo tried, but he wrote down the best of Geek knowledge that existed.

Both, I believe, considered “natural philosophy” to be a disinterested search for the truth. And they each were willing to let the Truth lead them where it would. Both refused to make their science the servant of the traditional gods that they each had to espouse.

Both tended toward believing in a universe governed ultimately by impersonal universal law, and that those laws could be understood through rational inquiry rather than revelation. Aristotle was too shrewd to criticize the Olympian gods, but he conceived of his God more as an “Unmoved Mover” or a “First Cause.”

In spite of that, Aristotle got a lot wrong, and the technology to do some of Galileo’s experiments (dropping balls of different weights off the Tower of Pisa) was really not that far beyond him. In the light of that, Aristotle’s limitations are a little puzzling.

26. What are the pros / advantages of Aristotelian argument​


Aristotelian argument, also known as classical argument, is a structured method of presenting a persuasive argument, originally developed by the philosopher Aristotle in his work "Rhetoric". Some of the key advantages of Aristotelian argument are:

Logical structure: Aristotelian argument follows a logical structure, with a clear introduction, body, and conclusion. This helps the audience to understand the argument and its main points.

Appeals to reason: Aristotelian argument relies on reason and evidence to support its claims. This can be effective in persuading an audience, as it appeals to their rational thinking and helps them to see the merits of the argument.

Recognized tradition: The Aristotelian argument has been widely used for over 2000 years, and is a recognized and respected method of presenting an argument. It is a traditional and reliable way to make a persuasive case.

Flexibility: The Aristotelian argument can be adapted to a wide range of topics and audiences, and can be used in both written and oral communication. This versatility makes it a useful tool in many different situations.

Emphasis on ethics: Aristotle believed that persuasion should be ethical and aimed at the greater good. This emphasis on ethics and morality can help to build trust and credibility with the audience, making the argument more persuasive.

Overall, the Aristotelian argument is an effective and time-tested method of making a persuasive case, with clear advantages in its logical structure, appeal to reason, recognized tradition, flexibility, and ethical emphasis.
