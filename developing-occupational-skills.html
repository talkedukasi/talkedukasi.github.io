6. What did the Japanese occupation contribute to the development of democracy inthe country?​


The Japanese occupation of the Philippines (Filipino: Pananakop ng mga Hapones sa Pilipinas; Japanese: 日本のフィリピン占領, romanized: Nihon no Firipin Senryō) occurred between 1942 and 1945, when Imperial Japan occupied the Commonwealth of the Philippines during World War II.

The invasion of the Philippines started on 8 December 1941, ten hours after the attack on Pearl Harbor. As at Pearl Harbor, American aircraft were severely damaged in the initial Japanese attack. Lacking air cover, the American Asiatic Fleet in the Philippines withdrew to Java on 12 December 1941. General Douglas MacArthur was ordered out, leaving his men at Corregidor on the night of 11 March 1942 for Australia, 4,000 km away. The 76,000 starving and sick American and Filipino defenders in Bataan surrendered on 9 April 1942, and were forced to endure the infamous Bataan Death March on which 7,000–10,000 died or were murdered. The 13,000 survivors on Corregidor surrendered on 6 May.

Japan occupied the Philippines for over three years, until the surrender of Japan. A highly effective guerilla campaign by Philippine resistance forces controlled sixty percent of the islands, mostly jungle and mountain areas. MacArthur supplied them by submarine, and sent reinforcements and officers. Filipinos remained loyal to the United States, partly because of the American guarantee of independence, and also because the Japanese had pressed large numbers of Filipinos into work details and even put young Filipino women into brothels.[1]

11. what is the trend of science and development in the philippines from pre-colonial, spanish reign, america occupation, and japanese invasion?


Hi, this is a very interesting topic in which I would like to help you with. Contrary to most people's belief, our ancestors were not ignorant before the colonization of any other nationals.

Before the Hispanic colonization, our ancestors have practices that are later on proven to be effective by Science. These practices are most likely developed through the trial method and experimentation. Our ancestors used herbs and plant materials as medicine and therapeutic practices to cure illnesses or to boost their energy and immune system.

Using what local resources they had, they were able to build waterways, boats and ships, houses that can last through storms and flooding, and the architectural wonder which is the Banaue Rice Terraces. With simple technology, they can mine from the earth and they can weave beautiful cloths. Education was the parents' responsibilities. They taught their children how to do household chores, to hunt, to plant and forage, and they even taught the Alibata or Baybayin system.

Since the Philippines was somewhat a port for merchants from China and other neighboring lands, the early Filipinos had a system for weighing, measuring, counting, and even a calendar.

When the Spaniards came, a formal system of education was introduced by the friars. Parish classrooms opened up a new world where students were taught the alphabet, music, religion, and arithmetic. The more affluent families started to send their sons to Europe for formal and higher education and these children brought back home knowledge they shared through their writings and in other forms.

The first university, University of Santo Tomas, was also established in the country. Sanitation, architecture and engineering of infrastructures, and medicine vastly improved.

When the Americans took over, we were introduced to English and the wonders of technology that greatly affected the way how we prepared food. Agriculture was also vastly improved since research centers were established and we learned how get more harvests in a year despite the climate changes throughout the year. Medicine and research centers for the study of tropical disease and illnesses were established and operated despite limited funds.

Not much progress was seen during the Japanese invasion since they were not here to provide guidance and help.

•Being courageous in standing up and being different from your friends: This skill is essential for building self-confidence and individuality. It involves having the courage to express yourself authentically, even if it means going against the opinions or actions of your peers.

•Developing self-esteem: Self-esteem is critical for positive mental health and well-being. It involves accepting yourself, feeling good about your abilities and qualities, and having confidence in your decisions and actions.

•Being true to yourself and avoiding the tendency to please others: This task involves being authentic and genuine in your relationships and avoiding the temptation to change who you are to fit in with others.
