Answer:

The human digestive system consists of the gastrointestinal tract plus the accessory organs of digestion (the tongue, salivary glands, pancreas, liver, and gallbladder). Digestion involves the breakdown of food into smaller and smaller components, until they can be absorbed and assimilated into the body. The process of digestion has three stages: the cephalic phase, the gastric phase, and the intestinal phase.

The first stage, the cephalic phase of digestion, begins with gastric secretions in response to the sight and smell of food. This stage includes the mechanical breakdown of food by chewing, and the chemical breakdown by digestive enzymes, that takes place in the mouth.

Saliva contains digestive enzymes called amylase, and lingual lipase, secreted by the salivary glands and serous glands on the tongue. The enzymes start to break down the food in the mouth. Chewing, in which the food is mixed with saliva, begins the mechanical process of digestion. This produces a bolus which can be swallowed down the esophagus to enter the stomach.

The second stage of digestion then begins in the stomach with the gastric phase of digestion. Here the food is further broken down by mixing with gastric acid until it passes into the duodenum, the first part of the small intestine.

The excretory system is a passive biological system that removes excess, unnecessary materials from the body fluids of an organism, so as to help maintain internal chemical homeostasis and prevent damage to the body. The dual function of excretory systems is the elimination of the waste products of metabolism and to drain the body of used up and broken down components in a liquid and gaseous state. In humans and other amniotes most of these substances leave the body as urine and to some degree exhalation, mammals also expel them through sweating.

22. Explain how the food passes in the digestive system then give a short description of why is it that certain parts of the digestive system should be in a parallel, intersecting, coinciding line​


Answer:

Explanation:

The digestive system is responsible for breaking down food into nutrients that can be absorbed by the body. The journey of food through the digestive system starts in the mouth and ends in the anus. Here is a brief explanation of how food passes through the digestive system:

Mouth: The process of digestion starts in the mouth, where food is chewed and mixed with saliva. Saliva contains enzymes that break down starches into simple sugars.

Esophagus: The food is then swallowed and travels down the esophagus, a muscular tube that connects the mouth to the stomach.

Stomach: The food enters the stomach, where it is mixed with digestive juices and enzymes that break down proteins. The stomach also churns the food to break it down into smaller particles.

Small intestine: The partially digested food then enters the small intestine, where it is further broken down and nutrients are absorbed into the bloodstream.

Large intestine: Any undigested food particles and waste products move into the large intestine, where water is absorbed and the waste material is prepared for elimination.

Rectum and Anus: Finally, the waste material is eliminated through the anus as feces.

Now, coming to the question of why certain parts of the digestive system should be in a parallel, intersecting, coinciding line, it is because this arrangement allows for an efficient and smooth flow of food and digestive juices through the system. If any part of the digestive system were to be out of line or not working properly, it could cause problems with digestion and absorption of nutrients, leading to various health issues. Therefore, the digestive system is designed in a specific way to ensure that food passes through it in a controlled and efficient manner.
