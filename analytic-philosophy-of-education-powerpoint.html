14. Explain how philosophy is logical and analytical.


Explanation:

Philosophy is a branch of knowledge that involves the critical examination of fundamental concepts such as reality, existence, knowledge, values, and language. It is logical and analytical because it employs rational and systematic methods of inquiry to explore these concepts. Philosophy uses logical and analytical tools such as deductive reasoning, induction, and critical analysis to investigate and evaluate arguments and theories.

Logical reasoning is a key element of philosophy because it involves identifying and evaluating the logical structure of arguments. Philosophers use deductive reasoning to identify and evaluate the validity of arguments, which involves drawing logical conclusions from premises. This helps to ensure that arguments are coherent and free from logical fallacies.

Analytical reasoning is another key aspect of philosophy, which involves breaking down complex concepts into smaller components and analyzing them in detail. Philosophers use analytical reasoning to identify assumptions, evaluate arguments, and clarify concepts. By breaking down complex ideas into smaller components, philosophers are able to better understand and analyze them, which helps to develop clearer and more rigorous arguments.

In summary, philosophy is logical and analytical because it uses logical and analytical tools to critically examine fundamental concepts and evaluate arguments. By employing these methods of inquiry, philosophers are able to develop clear, rigorous, and well-supported arguments, which are essential for advancing our understanding of the world around us

16. Philosophy in education


Answer:

The philosophy of education examines the goals, forms, methods, and meaning of education. The term is used to describe both fundamental philosophical analysis of these themes and the description or analysis of particular pedagogical approaches.

Philosophy of education is the branch of applied or practical philosophy concerned with the nature and aims of education and the philosophical problems arising from educational theory and practice. Because that practice is ubiquitous in and across human societies, its social and individual manifestations so varied, and its influence so profound, the subject is wide-ranging, involving issues in ethics and social/political philosophy, epistemology, metaphysics, philosophy of mind and language, and other areas of philosophy. Because it looks both inward to the parent discipline and outward to educational practice and the social, legal, and institutional contexts in which it takes place, philosophy of education concerns itself with both sides of the traditional theory/practice divide. Its subject matter includes both basic philosophical issues (e.g., the nature of the knowledge worth teaching, the character of educational equality and justice, etc.) and problems concerning specific educational policies and practices (e.g., the desirability of standardized curricula and testing, the social, economic, legal and moral dimensions of specific funding arrangements, the justification of curriculum decisions, etc.). In all this the philosopher of education prizes conceptual clarity, argumentative rigor, the fair-minded consideration of the interests of all involved in or affected by educational efforts and arrangements, and informed and well-reasoned valuation of educational aims and interventions.

19. philosophy of education


Philosophy of education is the branch of applied or practical philosophy concerned with the nature and aims of education and the philosophical problems arising from educational theory and practice. Because that practice is ubiquitous in and across human societies, its social and individual manifestations so varied, and its influence so profound, the subject is wide-ranging, involving issues in ethics and social/political philosophy, epistemology, metaphysics, philosophy of mind and language, and other areas of philosophy. Because it looks both inward to the parent discipline and outward to educational practice and the social, legal, and institutional contexts in which it takes place, philosophy of education concerns itself with both sides of the traditional theory/practice divide. Its subject matter includes both basic philosophical issues (e.g., the nature of the knowledge worth teaching, the character of educational equality and justice, etc.) and problems concerning specific educational policies and practices (e.g., the desirability of standardized curricula and testing, the social, economic, legal and moral dimensions of specific funding arrangements, the justification of curriculum decisions, etc.). In all this the philosopher of education prizes conceptual clarity, argumentative rigor, the fair-minded consideration of the interests of all involved in or affected by educational efforts and arrangements, and informed and well-reasoned valuation of educational aims and interventions.

Philosophy of education has a long and distinguished history in the Western philosophical tradition, from Socrates’ battles with the sophists to the present day. Many of the most distinguished figures in that tradition incorporated educational concerns into their broader philosophical agendas (Curren 2000, 2018; Rorty 1998). While that history is not the focus here, it is worth noting that the ideals of reasoned inquiry championed by Socrates and his descendants have long informed the view that education should foster in all students, to the extent possible, the disposition to seek reasons and the ability to evaluate them cogently, and to be guided by their evaluations in matters of belief, action and judgment. This view, that education centrally involves the fostering of reason or rationality, has with varying articulations and qualifications been embraced by most of those historical figures; it continues to be defended by contemporary philosophers of education as well (Scheffler 1973 [1989]; Siegel 1988, 1997, 2007, 2017). As with any philosophical thesis it is controversial; some dimensions of the controversy are explored below.

28. difference and alike between analytic and speculative Philosophy​


Answer:

analytic philosophy is a style of doing philosophy more united at this point in time by professional tendencies and inclinations than an actual agenda. It began when Bertrand Russell and G.E Moore, rebelled agaisnt the british idealism of their age. Interestingly enough, there wasnt one big thesis that united both philosophers, but they introduced what came to be the recognizing factors of a new school. Normally, analytic philosophy is contrasted with continental philosophy. (many also begin the separation in the early twentieth century, although some begin the separation with Kant, or immediately after, i believe this is a mistake).

Speculative philosophy is as old as philosophy itself and its a systematic attempt to analyze all reality by understanding its place in relation to other fields of inquiry. its emphasis is uniting rather than breaking down in to parts. Plato and Aristotle where speculative thinkers although the structures of their writing and the way analytic philosophers read the history of philosophy might belie this fact. the big medieval philosophers followed in Aristotle’s footsteps. The rationalists , german and british idealists, and as of lately process philosophers fit this camp as well.

Some, but definitely not all of continental philosophy can be classified as speculative. one reason is that continental philosophy after WW2 is in general skeptical of “grand speculative narratives” that speculative philosophy promises.

just like analytic philosophy, there is no one thesis that unites all speculative philosophers. this list however highlights some themes.
