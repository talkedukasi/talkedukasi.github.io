What is Chong-ak

Sujech'ion is a traditional wind group that performs a lengthy, rotating beat on the p'iri (bamboo flute) and taegum (big transverse bamboo flute). This ensemble is said to have existed for thousands of years. It illustrates the constant, continuous quality of traditional Korean music as a highly developed musical genre. Sujech'aon is like a long, broad river, fluid but full of strength, solemn yet vivacious. Foreigners who hear this music for the first time frequently describe it as a "heavenly sound" or "divine revelation," and it is true that this natural-sounding music appears to come from the distant past. Yongsan hoesang, which originated in Buddhist music, is now exclusively instrumental. This suite, which consists of nine parts, can be performed in its whole or in segments. It can be performed as a solo, string ensemble, or wind ensemble, depending on the instrumentation. The early pieces' sluggish speed produces a quiet, tranquil environment that eventually gives way to a faster tempo and more energetic sensation. This musical genre is utilized for solos performed by a variety of instruments and ensembles and is one of the most popular musical forms observed during traditional performances.Yomillak, a musical performance developed by King Sejong, was initially an extensive musical performance that featured singing and dancing, but the dance and vocal forms progressively declined, and it is now solely performed as an instrumental ensemble piece. Yomillak generated a variety of varieties, including the Yomillang Nyong, Yomillang Man, and Haeryong. Such variations were also observed in other traditional forms, indicating the Korean people's conservative nature and reverence for tradition. Traditionally, artists in Chong-ak music would not attempt to develop a wholly new genre. As a result, for centuries, a restricted repertory was repeated, gradually evolving over time to give rise to new varieties.
